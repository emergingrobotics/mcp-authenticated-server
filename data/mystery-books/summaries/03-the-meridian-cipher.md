# The Meridian Cipher

**Author:** Josephine Blackwell
**Published:** 1994
**Genre:** Mystery — Victorian Historical

---

## Overview

*The Meridian Cipher* is Josephine Blackwell's fourth novel and the one most critics agree announced her full arrival as a writer of serious literary ambition working within the mystery form. Published in 1994 to immediate acclaim, the book was shortlisted for the Gold Dagger Award and has never gone out of print. It is the story of a murder dressed up as an accident, a government riddled with deceit at its highest levels, and a decades-long act of betrayal so carefully concealed that it survives its architect's own investigation into the very crime he committed. Blackwell spent three years researching the mechanics of Victorian cryptography, the culture of the Foreign Office in the late 1880s, and the particular anxieties of a British establishment that believed itself invulnerable even as it was being quietly hollowed out.

The novel opens on a grey November morning in 1887 when the body of Aldous Kempe — senior cryptographer to Her Majesty's Foreign Office, Fellow of the Royal Cryptographic Society, and one of the most respected technical minds in Whitehall — is pulled from the Thames near Blackfriars Bridge. His pockets contain, among the usual effects of a gentleman of his station, eleven sodden fragments of paper bearing a sequence of alphanumeric groups in a cipher that no one at the Foreign Office can immediately identify. The official verdict is drowning, ruled accidental by the coroner's jury. But the fragments speak a different language entirely, and it falls to the novel's protagonist, Inspector Cora Naismith of the Metropolitan Police's nascent Special Enquiries branch — a woman whose appointment to that role was itself a minor scandal — to read what they are trying to say.

What *The Meridian Cipher* ultimately delivers is not merely a puzzle to be solved but an anatomy of institutional loyalty and its corruptions. Blackwell is interested in the way organisations like the Foreign Office create identities so total — so deeply fused with the self — that betraying the institution and remaining devoted to it can coexist in a single person across twenty years. The book's moral weight rests not on the moment of murder but on the accumulated weight of Reginald Foss's choices over two decades, and the question Blackwell leaves hanging after the final page: whether a man who believed he served a higher diplomatic order was a traitor or simply a man who drew his loyalties differently than the crown demanded.

## Setting

London in November 1887 is rendered in *The Meridian Cipher* with an almost suffocating specificity. Blackwell had clearly walked every street she describes, or had access to maps and memoirs detailed enough to produce the same effect. The novel's atmosphere is that of a city on the edge of several anxieties simultaneously: the anniversary of the Gunpowder Plot had just passed, the Fenian bombing campaign of the early decade had left its marks on public memory, and the approach of the Queen's Golden Jubilee year cast a complicated light over questions of empire, loyalty, and the state apparatus that maintained both. The Thames itself is a constant presence — not as romantic backdrop but as working infrastructure, industrial artery, and receptacle for the city's waste, including, on occasion, its inconvenient dead.

Whitechapel in this novel is not yet the Whitechapel of the Ripper autumn of 1888 — that horror is one year away — but it is already a neighbourhood under surveillance, already associated in the minds of the establishment with foreign radicals, anarchist printing presses, and the dangerous ideas being imported from the continent alongside the immigrants who carried them. Inspector Naismith moves through Whitechapel's courts and lodging houses with a pragmatic ease that marks her as unusual among her colleagues, most of whom view the area as enemy territory. It is here that she finds the anarchist cell that Special Branch has been watching for months, operating out of a chandler's cellar on Hanbury Street, and it is here that the novel's first great red herring takes root in fertile ground. The neighbourhood's poverty, density, and political volatility make it the obvious place to look for Kempe's killer — which is precisely why Blackwell places the real answer somewhere else entirely.

Whitehall in 1887 is depicted as a place of vast institutional self-confidence shading into arrogance. The Foreign Office building on King Charles Street, completed only eighteen years earlier, was still new enough to project imperial ambition in its stonework and cornices. Blackwell gives meticulous attention to its internal geography — the basement rooms where the cipher room operated, the first-floor corridors where senior staff had their offices, the small reading room off the main staircase where officers sometimes took lunch alone when they had matters to turn over quietly in their minds. Aldous Kempe's office, she tells us, smelled of india rubber and coal smoke and the particular kind of anxiety that comes from knowing things that cannot be written down. Deputy Undersecretary Reginald Foss occupied the office three doors down the same corridor, and the route between them was worn slightly smoother than the surrounding parquet, a detail Naismith notices and records in her notebook without yet understanding why.

The private clubs of Pall Mall provide the novel's third major space, and Blackwell uses them with surgical intent. The Terminus Club, where Foss is a member and where much of the novel's social manoeuvring takes place, is a fictional establishment drawn from detailed study of the Reform and the Athenaeum. It is a place where information moves in the guise of conversation, where the candid remark over port is never truly candid, and where every man in the room is aware of every other man's position in the hierarchy without that hierarchy ever being named aloud. Kempe was not a club man — he found them stifling, his diary notes — but Foss was entirely at home there, and it is at the Terminus Club that he first met the Austrian contact who would recruit him in 1867. Blackwell returns to the club repeatedly throughout the novel, not because anything dramatic happens there but because it represents the texture of the world Foss was protecting — and the world he was simultaneously betraying.

## Main Characters

**Inspector Cora Naismith** is thirty-four years old when the novel begins, a widow of three years whose husband, a surgeon named Thomas Naismith, died of typhoid contracted from a patient he was treating in St Bartholomew's. She joined the Metropolitan Police in an administrative capacity in 1882, when her husband's death left her financially independent but purposeless, and worked her way — through a combination of genuine aptitude and the patronage of Commissioner James Monro, who was unusual among senior officers in his willingness to use unconventional tools — into the Special Enquiries branch, which handled cases too sensitive or too irregular for ordinary detective work. Her appointment was greeted with contempt by most of her male colleagues, with cautious respect by a few, and with active hostility by Detective Sergeant Walter Prout, who had expected the position for himself and never forgave her for it. Naismith is not depicted as remarkable in any supernatural or implausible sense. She is observant, methodical, and possessed of a social mobility — she can pass as working class in Whitechapel and as middle-class professional in Whitehall — that is her greatest practical asset. Her grief for Thomas is not sentimental but structural: it has changed the architecture of her inner life, and Blackwell suggests that her unusual clarity of perception is partly a product of someone who has decided, after great loss, to see things as they actually are rather than as she would prefer them to be. Her friendship with Aldous Kempe, whom she met through a Royal Society lecture on frequency analysis three years before his death, is the emotional core of the novel, and his murder is not merely a professional case for her but a personal wound that sharpens and sometimes distorts her judgment.

Naismith's voice is the novel's primary instrument, and Blackwell writes her internal monologue with a precision that never tips into the anachronistically modern. She thinks in the idioms of her era and class while seeing past them — a difficult balance to sustain across three hundred pages, and one Blackwell largely achieves. Her method of investigation is conversational and lateral: she does not interrogate so much as listen, and she reads omissions as carefully as statements. The key insight that eventually breaks the case — that Reginald Foss's account of his movements on the night of Kempe's death contains a single gap of forty minutes that he has filled with a verifiable lie — comes not from a dramatic confrontation but from a quiet afternoon in the registry of the Terminus Club, where she checks the date of Foss's attendance against the log book and finds the record has been altered. It is an unglamorous discovery, made by a woman sitting alone with a ledger, and Blackwell presents it as the most consequential moment in the book.

**Deputy Undersecretary Reginald Foss** is fifty-one years old, a tall, silver-haired career Foreign Office man of impeccable surface reputation. He is the novel's villain, but Blackwell resists the temptation to make him monstrous. He is intelligent, genuinely charming, capable of real friendship, and by his own lights sincere in his conviction that the information he passed to Vienna over twenty years served the cause of European stability better than the blinkered nationalism he observed daily in his colleagues. He was recruited in 1867 during a posting to Vienna, where he fell under the influence of a Hungarian-born diplomat named Count Miklós Esterházy-Wirth, who presented the sharing of intelligence not as treason but as a form of enlightened diplomacy, a private channel that prevented misunderstanding between great powers. Foss believed this — genuinely, at first, and then by necessity — and constructed an elaborate internal justification that allowed him to continue believing it even as the information he passed became increasingly operational rather than merely political. His relationship with Kempe is the novel's most painful element. They had been friends for twenty-two years. They ate lunch together three times a week. Foss was the person Kempe told first when he began to suspect there was a mole in the cipher room's supply chain — a detail that signed Kempe's death warrant, because Foss could not allow the investigation to continue once Kempe had produced a fragment analysis that would eventually point, through a chain of deductive steps, directly at him.

The murder itself — Kempe drugged with laudanum dissolved in his evening whisky at a meeting Foss arranged under the pretence of discussing the investigation's progress, then walked to the Embankment and eased into the river — is rendered with devastating understatement. Blackwell does not describe it until the novel's penultimate chapter, when Foss's written confession is read aloud, and the gap between the warmth of his earlier scenes with Kempe and the clinical efficiency of what he describes produces a vertigo that is the book's single most powerful effect.

**Aldous Kempe** is present in the novel almost entirely through absence and record. He is dead before the first chapter ends, and everything we know of him comes from his diary, his professional papers, the testimony of colleagues, and Naismith's memories of their friendship. He was fifty-three, a bachelor, a man whose entire identity had been built around cryptographic work that he regarded as a form of art as much as a technical discipline. His discovery of the mole was not a deliberate investigation but an accidental consequence of a pattern-recognition exercise he was conducting on old diplomatic cables: he noticed that certain intercepts from the late 1860s and early 1870s bore the hallmarks of having been pre-processed — subtly structured — in a way that suggested the enciphering party had access to the British key schedule in advance. He spent six months working through the implications before he was certain enough to bring his suspicions to Foss. The fragments found in his coat pockets are the final stage of his analysis: a partial reconstruction of the original leaked material and a sequence of identifying markers that, fully assembled, would name Foss unambiguously. He had not yet assembled them into a readable document. He never got the chance.

**Detective Sergeant Walter Prout** occupies the novel's comic-bitter register: a competent, unimaginative policeman who resents Naismith with a consistency that never quite tips into comedy because Blackwell makes it clear that his resentment is institutional as much as personal, and that it does real damage to the investigation. He pursues the anarchist cell with territorial intensity throughout the novel's middle section, arresting three men on circumstantial grounds and refusing to re-examine his theory even when the evidence grows ambiguous. He is not corrupt, merely blinkered, and Blackwell is careful to give him one moment of genuine professional grace — near the end, when he accepts Naismith's reconstruction of the case without public argument — that redeems him just enough to make his earlier obstructions feel tragic rather than merely irritating.

## Plot Summary

The novel opens on the morning of 14 November 1887. A waterman named George Fitch pulls what he takes for a bundle of sodden cloth from the river near Blackfriars and discovers instead the body of a well-dressed gentleman. The body is identified within hours as Aldous Kempe by a Foreign Office junior clerk named Morton Haig, who is sent to the morgue and returns pale and shaken. The coroner, a Dr Samuel Peate, notes water in the lungs and no obvious signs of violence and recommends an accidental drowning verdict. The case would have ended there except that Haig, in the process of searching Kempe's pockets for identification, discovered the cipher fragments and, uncertain what to do with them, had the presence of mind to hand them to Special Branch rather than simply filing them with the personal effects. It is Special Branch that routes the case to Naismith.

Naismith's first act is to visit Kempe's office in the Foreign Office, where she is received with barely concealed hostility by nearly everyone except Reginald Foss, who greets her with warmth, expresses his grief for his old friend with what appears to be genuine feeling, and places himself at her disposal for whatever enquiries she may have. His helpfulness is so immediate and so complete that Naismith notes it in her journal that evening — not suspiciously, but as a data point. She is also struck by the fact that Foss was the last person, so far as anyone knows, to have seen Kempe alive: they had met for their usual Wednesday evening drink at the Reform Club, parting at approximately nine o'clock. Kempe was found the following morning. The path from the Reform Club to Blackfriars along the Embankment is a walk Kempe was known to take occasionally. Everything is consistent with a solitary late-night walk, a stumble, a drowning.

Naismith begins assembling Kempe's recent movements and discovers that in the six weeks before his death he had been working unusual hours — arriving before seven, leaving after eight, behaviour that his colleagues attributed to a project he was characteristically cagey about. She obtains access to his desk diary and finds a series of entries in a personal shorthand she cannot initially read. She brings these to a young cryptographic assistant named Oliver Sparke, the youngest member of Kempe's team, who deciphers enough of the entries to establish that Kempe had been conducting what he called a "provenance audit" of old diplomatic intercepts — comparing them against key schedules from the same period to look for anomalies in the enciphering. The purpose of such an audit, Sparke explains, would be to detect evidence of key compromise: in other words, to find out whether someone with access to British keys had been sharing them with a foreign power.

The fragments from Kempe's coat now take on their full significance. Naismith has them partially reconstructed by Sparke and by a retired cipher specialist she contacts through the Royal Cryptographic Society, a formidably sharp woman in her seventies named Helena Morrice. The fragments are not a complete message but a cipher analyst's working notes — markers identifying the characteristics of a leak that can be dated to no earlier than 1867 and no later than 1871, and that would have required access to both the British key schedule and the Austrian response patterns to construct. The number of people with that precise combination of access is, Morrice tells Naismith, very small. She does not name Foss. She does not need to, quite yet.

The investigation's first major detour arrives in the form of the Hanbury Street anarchist cell. Special Branch has been watching a group of seven men — three Russian émigrés, two Poles, and two British-born radicals — who have been meeting in the cellar of a chandler's shop run by a man named Viktor Brandt. Prout obtains information — subsequently revealed to be fabricated by an informant with a personal grievance — suggesting that Kempe had attended one of the cell's meetings and that he may have been killed to prevent him from reporting what he saw. Prout arrests Brandt and two of the émigrés. Naismith attends the interrogations and concludes almost immediately that the cell had no contact with Kempe and that the information was worthless, but she cannot dislodge Prout from his theory and the arrests dominate the press coverage for ten days, creating a satisfying narrative about foreign anarchists and British espionage that distracts public attention from the Foreign Office entirely.

The second red herring appears in the person of Arthur Machen-Tully, a senior cryptographer who had been passed over for the position Kempe held and who had never reconciled himself to the slight. Machen-Tully is bitter, indiscreet, and possessed of a detailed knowledge of Kempe's professional methods that makes him superficially plausible as a suspect with both motive and means. He also has the misfortune of having been seen arguing loudly with Kempe in the corridor outside the cipher room approximately two weeks before the death — an argument witnessed by three people and concerning a methodological dispute about the proper treatment of historical cables. Naismith interviews him twice. He is hostile, evasive about his whereabouts on the night of the fourteenth (he was, it eventually emerges, conducting a clandestine affair with a junior colleague's wife and could not say so), and says several things about Kempe that are unkind enough to seem like genuine animosity. It takes Naismith until the novel's midpoint to establish that his hostility is professional and emotional — real enough, but insufficient, and pointing in the wrong direction.

The mysterious woman — a figure seen walking with Kempe along the Embankment at approximately eight-thirty on the evening of the fourteenth, roughly thirty minutes before the window in which Foss claims they parted — proves to be Eleanor Fitch, the estranged daughter of the same waterman who found the body. She had encountered Kempe by chance near Waterloo Bridge and walked with him for perhaps twenty minutes while he was clearly distracted and preoccupied. She had nothing material to contribute to the investigation, but her presence in Kempe's final hour, combined with the coincidence of her surname, sends Naismith down a blind alley for the better part of a week during which she investigates a possible romantic connection between Kempe and this woman that never existed.

The case breaks open in late December when Naismith, acting on a detail in Kempe's diary that she has been slowly decoding with Sparke's help, visits the Terminus Club registry to verify Foss's account of his movements on a particular evening in October — the night Kempe had recorded a private meeting with his contact in the audit inquiry, a meeting whose location he noted only as "T.C." The registry log shows Foss was present that evening. But the log also shows, in slightly different ink and with letter forms that Naismith, on Morrice's advice, has shown to a graphological analyst, that the entry for the evening of the fourteenth — the night of Kempe's death — has been altered. The original entry has been struck through and rewritten to show Foss leaving at nine o'clock, when the original, faintly legible under close examination, reads seven-forty. Forty minutes. Time enough to walk with Kempe to the water's edge and return before anyone noticed.

Foss is confronted not dramatically but quietly, in the small reading room of the Foreign Office where he and Kempe used to take lunch. Naismith presents her findings without theatrics. Foss listens, asks three clarifying questions, and then says, in a voice she later describes as sounding like a man reading from a prepared text: "I would like to write a statement." His confession is eighteen pages long. It begins: "I have served two masters for twenty years and told myself that in doing so I served only one: the cause of a Europe that does not destroy itself." It ends with the sentence: "I killed Aldous because he was going to find what I had spent half my life hiding, and in the moment I chose to protect that life over his, I understood that I had already lost everything worth protecting in it."

## Mystery Elements

The central mystery of *The Meridian Cipher* is not simply who killed Aldous Kempe but the layered question of how a trusted colleague and close friend could be simultaneously a murderer and a man of genuine warmth and loyalty. Blackwell constructs the puzzle on two levels: the factual level of who was where on the night of the fourteenth and what their motive was, and the psychological level of how a human being sustains a double life across twenty years without it destroying him — or rather, how the destruction accumulates invisibly until it produces a moment like the one on the Embankment. The cipher fragments are the novel's central physical clue, but the most important clue in the book is the altered registry entry, which is not found until late in the narrative precisely because it requires Naismith to have assembled enough of the picture to know where to look.

The killer is Reginald Foss, Deputy Undersecretary in the Foreign Office's intelligence division and Kempe's closest professional friend and personal confidant. His motive is survival — specifically, the survival of an identity he has constructed over twenty years as both a respected British official and a covert source for the Austro-Hungarian intelligence service. He was recruited in Vienna in 1867 by Count Miklós Esterházy-Wirth under ideological cover: Esterházy-Wirth argued persuasively that the balance of power in Europe was too important to be left to nationalistic governments, and that men of sufficient education and perspective had an obligation to operate beyond their formal loyalties when the stability of the continent was at stake. Foss was thirty-one, intellectually vain, newly arrived at his first major posting, and susceptible to this argument in a way he never entirely admitted, even in his confession. Over the following two decades he provided Vienna with British diplomatic key schedules, selected intelligence assessments, and — in three specific instances that he describes with careful minimisation in his written statement — the identities of British agents operating in the Austro-Hungarian sphere. His contact changed four times over the period. By 1887 he was passing information not to a charming philosopher-diplomat but to a professional intelligence officer named Horvath whose methods were considerably less idealistic. Foss had long since ceased to believe in the framing that had recruited him, but he had no exit: twenty years of treason does not have a retirement option.

The red herrings in the novel are constructed with particular care because Blackwell is committed to making each of them plausible rather than merely convenient. The Hanbury Street anarchist cell is the most structurally important false lead because it offers an explanation that is politically satisfying to almost everyone involved in the investigation. Special Branch wants the cell to be responsible because it justifies their surveillance operation. The press wants the cell to be responsible because it fits a narrative of foreign subversion that was culturally dominant in 1887. Even Naismith, who does not believe in the anarchist theory, can see how completely it insulates the Foreign Office from scrutiny — and suspects, correctly, that this convenience is not accidental. The fabricated informant information that Prout receives was, the novel suggests in a late passage, possibly arranged by Foss himself through an intermediary, though this is never conclusively established and Blackwell is wise enough to leave it as a shadow rather than a certainty.

Arthur Machen-Tully is a more intimate red herring and in some ways a crueller one, because his bitterness is genuine and his behaviour under questioning is genuinely suspicious, and Naismith's investigation into him is not a mistake — it is the correct response to the evidence available. That he was conducting an affair he could not disclose, and that this affair explains his false alibi and evasive manner, is a piece of novelistic plotting that Blackwell executes with characteristic restraint: it is revealed not through a dramatic confession but through a letter Naismith intercepts via a Post Office contact, a letter from a woman who signs herself only "M" and who expresses panic about whether Walter will be told. The moment Naismith reads it, Machen-Tully recedes as a suspect, but the time spent investigating him has not been wasted: it has established the culture of concealment within the Foreign Office more thoroughly than any direct approach could have done.

Eleanor Fitch, the woman seen with Kempe on the Embankment, functions as a different kind of misdirection. She is not a deliberate red herring planted by the killer but one constructed by circumstance and the narrative logic of mystery fiction itself: a woman walking alone with a man at night, carrying a name that links her to the discovery of the body, must be significant. Blackwell uses the reader's expectation of significance against them here. Eleanor has nothing to do with the murder. She is simply a woman who happened to be present during a portion of Kempe's final evening and who observed that he was distracted and kept looking behind him — which is the most useful thing anyone tells Naismith, because it confirms that Kempe knew by that point that he was in danger, and had likely worked out from whom.

The narrative's principal mechanism for maintaining suspense and misdirecting suspicion is Foss's own active involvement in the investigation. He volunteers information, makes himself available, expresses grief publicly and apparently sincerely, and — most damningly in retrospect — subtly steers Naismith's attention toward Machen-Tully in their early conversations by mentioning, with every appearance of reluctance, that Kempe had found Machen-Tully's recent behaviour erratic and threatening. This is all recontextualised with great force once the confession is read: every scene in which Foss appears as a helpful, grieving friend becomes a scene of sustained performance, and the reader is invited to revisit the entire novel in light of what they now know. Blackwell achieves this recontextualisation without dishonesty — everything Foss says and does is consistent with his guilt — but the human warmth she allows him in his scenes with Naismith is genuine enough that the betrayal lands with full weight.

The moment of revelation is deliberately procedural. There is no confrontation scene, no dramatic unmasking. Naismith presents the altered registry entry, the graphological report, and a reconstructed timeline to a senior Foreign Office official before she approaches Foss at all, ensuring that she cannot be easily dismissed or suppressed. When she meets Foss in the reading room, he already knows that the game is over — she can see it in the way he has arranged the chairs — and what follows is not a battle of wills but a kind of mutual acknowledgement. His decision to write a full confession rather than force a trial is understood by Naismith, and later by the reader, as the last act of the friendship he destroyed: he spares the investigation the difficulty of proving what he already knows they can prove, and he provides enough detail about his contacts and methods to make the confession useful to the intelligence service. Whether this constitutes honour or merely the final performance of a man who has always understood how to manage the impressions of those around him is left to the reader to determine.

## Themes

- **The architecture of institutional loyalty and its corruptions.** *The Meridian Cipher* is fundamentally a novel about what institutions ask of the individuals who serve them — and what those individuals tell themselves when they decide to serve two at once. Foss's self-justification, which he has refined over twenty years into something he can articulate with genuine elegance, is that the Foreign Office's loyalty to the British state was always a narrower and more parochial thing than his own loyalty to European stability. Blackwell takes this argument seriously enough to give it real intellectual weight before she dismantles it, showing that the logical structure of Foss's position has been gradually hollowed out by the operational reality of what he was actually providing. By 1887 he is not serving European stability; he is keeping himself out of prison. The idealism that recruited him is the period costume in which a much more ordinary self-interest has dressed itself.

- **The epistemology of trust.** The novel asks, persistently and without comfortable resolution, how we know whether someone we trust is trustworthy. Naismith trusted Kempe. Kempe trusted Foss. The reader, for most of the novel, has no particular reason not to trust Foss either — Blackwell withholds nothing dishonestly, but she deploys warmth and credibility with such precision that Foss is the last person a first-time reader suspects. The novel suggests that trust is not a rational calculation but a kind of narrative commitment: we decide to believe the story someone presents and then interpret subsequent evidence through that lens. Naismith's investigative method is partly a systematic attempt to postpone this commitment — to remain in the position of not-yet-trusting — and the novel implies that this skill, which makes her a good detective, also makes her someone for whom intimacy is permanently difficult.

- **Grief as a mode of perception.** Naismith's widowhood is not incidental to the story but central to her method. Having lost her husband to a disease that spared no certainty — that took him between a Tuesday when he was fine and a Friday when he was not — she has developed a particular attentiveness to absence and to the ways in which the presence of someone familiar can be maintained as a screen against what is actually happening. She recognises in Foss's performance of grief a quality she knows from the inside: the way grief gives the griever permission to behave strangely, to be forgiven for distance or distraction or excessive helpfulness. She cannot quite articulate this as suspicion for most of the novel, but it is what keeps her watching him even when the anarchist theory is most persuasive.

- **Cryptography as metaphor.** The professional world of Kempe and Foss is one in which meaning is always concealed beneath a surface that must be laboriously decoded, and Blackwell uses this as a structural metaphor without ever making it heavy-handed. The cipher fragments that launch the investigation are the novel's most literal expression of this theme — a message that contains the truth but can only be read if you possess the right keys — but the metaphor ramifies throughout. Foss's confession is itself a kind of cipher: a document that says one thing on its surface (a man taking responsibility, demonstrating honour in defeat) and contains within it, for a reader attentive enough, the shape of a personality that has spent decades encoding itself against discovery.

- **The gender of authority in Victorian public life.** Naismith's position in the Metropolitan Police is a structural anomaly, and Blackwell does not let the novel forget this. The resistance she encounters — from Prout, from Foreign Office officials who address themselves to the uniformed constable accompanying her rather than to her, from a coroner who twice asks her to wait outside while he speaks with a male colleague — is presented not as villainy but as the ordinary texture of an institutional culture that does not have a category for what she is. Her effectiveness comes partly from the fact that she is consistently underestimated, and partly from the fact that she has learned to use the margins of institutional space — the registries, the archivists, the junior staff who are never themselves taken seriously — as sources of intelligence that her more conventionally powerful colleagues overlook. The novel is not polemical on this point; it simply shows the work, and trusts the reader to draw the appropriate conclusions.

- **The long cost of small decisions.** Foss's catastrophe is not built from one enormous act of treachery but from a sequence of small decisions, each of which seemed manageable at the time and each of which made the next one harder to refuse. The logic of his situation — once started, continuation was always less dangerous than stopping — is presented with enough granularity that the reader can follow the chain, link by link, from the idealistic young diplomat in Vienna in 1867 to the methodical killer on the Embankment in 1887. Blackwell is not interested in excusing this progression, but she is insistently interested in making it comprehensible, because she understands that incomprehensible evil is not actually frightening — only the evil we can follow the logic of reaches us where we live.
