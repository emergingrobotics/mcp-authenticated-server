# The Glass Archivist

**Author:** Miriam Strand
**Published:** 2019
**Genre:** Mystery — Contemporary Locked-Room

---

## Overview

*The Glass Archivist* is Miriam Strand's sixth novel and her most formally audacious: a locked-room mystery set almost entirely within the walls of a single private institution in Edinburgh, it operates simultaneously as a rigorous puzzle, a meditation on the nature of authenticity and forgery, and an examination of the specific damage done by long-term institutional complicity in a powerful man's corruption. Published in 2019, the novel arrived at a cultural moment acutely attuned to questions about the price paid by people who work within abusive power structures, and its reception reflected that attunement — it won the Crime Writers' Association's Gold Dagger, was longlisted for the Baillie Gifford Prize, and generated an unusual volume of commentary from rare book specialists and conservation scientists who praised its technical accuracy while debating, sometimes heatedly, the plausibility of its central forgery.

The novel begins at dawn on a February morning when Raquel Ife, the Calloway Archive's junior conservator, arrives early for work and finds the vault door sealed from the inside in a manner that the Archive's security protocols identify as a lockout — a state the system is designed to enter only when someone inside has engaged the override. She calls the head archivist, Dr. Petra Voss. She calls the Archive's director of operations. By eight o'clock, when the door is opened by the Archive's security contractor using the master override sequence, the body of Magnus Calloway — the Archive's founder and sole owner, a billionaire who had spent thirty years assembling what was widely described as the finest private collection of early printed books in Scotland — is found seated at the reading table in the centre of the vault, a glass of Armagnac at his elbow, a copy of the contested First Folio open before him, and no visible cause of death. The doors had been locked from the inside. There were no other exits. The climate control system had been running normally all night. Magnus Calloway was alone in the vault when he died, and he appeared, at first inspection, simply to have stopped.

What *The Glass Archivist* does with this premise is characteristic of Strand's best work: it refuses to let the puzzle dominate the human substance of the story. The locked-room mechanism — and it is a genuine locked-room, the solution to which involves no supernatural elements and no physical impossibility — is ultimately less interesting than what the room contained and what it meant to the people responsible for maintaining it. Strand is a former museum cataloguer with a postgraduate degree in the history of the book, and her authority over the material is absolute. The forgery at the centre of the novel is described with enough technical detail to be genuinely alarming — it is not a crude fake but a work of extraordinary skill and scholarship — and the question of how Dr. Petra Voss authenticated it, under what pressures, and what she chose to do with her knowledge of her own error, forms the emotional spine around which the mystery's bones are arranged.

## Setting

The Calloway Archive occupies a converted Georgian townhouse on a private road off the Canongate in Edinburgh, a building whose original purpose was the home of a nineteenth-century shipping merchant and whose current purpose — the housing and preservation of approximately twelve thousand rare and antiquarian books, along with associated manuscripts, maps, and ephemera — is announced only by a small brass plate beside the front door reading "The Calloway Archive — Private. Appointment Required." Edinburgh is a city with a peculiar relationship to institutional seriousness: it takes its collections and its scholarship with a gravity that can shade into self-congratulation, and Magnus Calloway had exploited this cultural tendency expertly. The Archive was not merely a private library; it was a statement about a certain kind of wealth and a certain kind of ambition — the ambition to be taken seriously by the scholarly world on the scholarly world's own terms, which required not just possessing rare books but employing people qualified to care for them and admitting, on carefully controlled terms, the researchers who wished to study them.

The vault is the novel's central architectural space and Strand describes it with the precision of someone who has stood in rooms like it. It occupies the building's entire basement footprint: sixty square metres of climate-controlled space maintained at fifteen degrees Celsius and forty-five percent relative humidity, its walls lined with custom-built acid-free shelving, its ceiling fitted with LED lighting calibrated to produce minimal UV output, its single point of entry a steel-reinforced door with a biometric lock, an electronic keypad override, and — this is the detail that matters — an interior manual deadbolt that can be engaged from within to prevent the electronic system from being overridden externally. The deadbolt is present for what the Archive's original security contractor described in his design notes as "enhanced preservation integrity in the event of external security compromise" — it was meant to allow a person trapped inside by a security system failure to prevent anyone from entering until help could be summoned by phone. It was never intended to be used the way it was used on the night Magnus Calloway died.

The reading room immediately above the vault is where most of the novel's interviews and depositions take place: a long room with tall windows overlooking a walled garden, a central table capable of seating twelve, and display cases along the north wall containing items from the Archive's collection that Calloway had deemed suitable for general viewing. It is a room designed to communicate gravitas and exclusivity simultaneously, and Strand uses it as a stage for the social performances of the novel's suspects — the careful self-presentation of people who are aware they are being observed and assessed. The detective investigating the case, Detective Inspector Saoirse Mullane of Police Scotland's Major Investigations Unit, sits at the head of the reading room table for most of her interviews, a position that Calloway's assistant noted he invariably occupied himself and that Mullane chose, whether consciously or not, with the result that she inhabits the room with an authority that slightly unnerves her interviewees. Strand observes this dynamic with dry appreciation.

Edinburgh itself is present in the novel more atmospherically than literally — the city's weather (a raw February with intermittent sleet), its particular density of academic and institutional life, the proximity of the university and the National Library and the half-dozen other collecting institutions that formed the ecosystem in which the Calloway Archive operated. Strand's Edinburgh is a city in which reputation is currency, where the scholarly community is small enough that everyone knows everyone and professional rivalries have the intimacy of personal ones, and where the sudden death of a major collector opens a vacuum that the surrounding institutions immediately begin, with barely concealed appetite, to assess for their own purposes. The Archive's situation — privately owned, without an endowment or institutional affiliation, dependent entirely on Calloway's continued willingness to fund it — is understood by everyone in this community as precarious, and the question of what happens to it now dominates the background of the investigation in a way that shades the motivations of almost everyone who enters the reading room table.

## Main Characters

**Detective Inspector Saoirse Mullane** is forty-two, Irish-born but twenty years in Scotland, a career detective who came to Major Investigations from a background in financial crime. Her experience with financial fraud gives her an unusual skill set for a murder investigation: she is exceptionally good at following the movements of money and at reading the behaviour of people who have something to hide about their professional conduct — people who have learned, through years of careful practice, to present a surface of normalcy over a foundation that will not bear close examination. She is less instinctively focused on the physical details of crime scenes than her colleagues and initially underweights the locked-room problem, spending the early chapters of her investigation on the social and financial architecture of the Archive before she turns back to the vault itself. This is presented not as a mistake but as a methodological preference: she has learned from financial crime that the most important things are usually not in the room but in the records, and that understanding who stands to gain is often more productive than understanding who had access.

Mullane's characterisation carries a deliberate restraint. Strand gives her a life outside the investigation — a long-term partner named Declan who is a secondary school music teacher, a complicated relationship with her mother in Cork, a running habit that she abandons during the investigation and resumes only in the novel's final pages — but these details serve primarily to establish interiority rather than to provide subplot. What makes Mullane interesting is her professional manner: she is courteous to the point of lulling, a quality that several of her interviewees mistake for a lack of sharpness and that Strand allows to do serious investigative work. Her interview with Dr. Petra Voss three days into the investigation — a long, apparently discursive conversation that begins with a discussion of the Archive's cataloguing practices and ends with Voss having said three things she did not intend to say — is the novel's most technically accomplished scene, and it is accomplished entirely through the deployment of patience. Mullane says almost nothing that could be identified as a probing question. She simply keeps the conversation going, in the tone of someone who is genuinely interested in the history of the institution, until the gaps in Voss's account become too wide to ignore.

**Dr. Petra Voss** is the novel's most fully realised character and in many respects its protagonist, despite the fact that she is also its killer. She is fifty-six, German-born, trained at the Institut für Stadtgeschichte in Frankfurt and at Leiden, and she has been the Calloway Archive's head archivist for fourteen years. She is a scholar of genuine distinction — her published work on the transmission history of early English printed books is cited extensively in the field — and her commitment to the Archive's collections is not performative but authentic. She loves the books. She loves the work of caring for them. This is what makes her situation, as Strand constructs it, so exquisitely painful: the person responsible for the Archive's intellectual integrity has spent seven years living with the knowledge that its most celebrated acquisition — the item around which Calloway had built the Archive's international reputation — is a forgery that she authenticated.

The history of Voss's involvement in the First Folio authentication is told in fragments across the novel, assembled from her own account (initially partial and self-exculpatory, eventually complete), from contemporaneous correspondence that Mullane finds in the Archive's administrative files, and from a taped interview that Calloway had made in 2016 in which he discussed the Folio acquisition in terms that are carefully ambiguous about the authentication process. In 2012, Voss conducted a preliminary examination of the volume and produced a report expressing significant reservations about two quire signatures and the paper's watermark evidence. Calloway had the volume re-examined by a consultant paper historian who produced a report supporting authenticity. Voss subsequently produced a revised assessment that endorsed authentication. The revised assessment was, she later acknowledges, wrong — she knew it was wrong when she wrote it, or at least she knew that she had suppressed doubts that she had not resolved, under a combination of pressure from Calloway and the seductive weight of wanting the Folio to be real. What she did not know at the time was that Calloway had the taped record of her preliminary doubts. He had been recording his archivists' conversations, a practice she discovered in 2017 when he made explicit to her, without quite stating it as a threat, that he was in possession of material that would destroy her professional reputation if it became public.

The blackmail — which Calloway never called by that name and which Voss never directly confronted him about — operated for five years as a condition of her employment: she remained at the Archive, did excellent work, and did not publicly raise questions about the Folio's authenticity. When Calloway announced the private auction of the volume, she understood that what had been a private arrangement was about to become a public fraud. The buyer — an American technology billionaire named Christopher Dunbar-Knox, paying fourteen million pounds for what he believed to be the only copy of the First Folio in private hands in Scotland — would be defrauded on the basis of an authentication report bearing her name. She requested a private meeting with Calloway for the evening of the auction. He agreed. She brought with her the results of a spectral analysis she had commissioned privately, at her own expense, from a university contact, analysis that she had not shared with Calloway. The results confirmed her original doubts and added several new ones. She asked him to halt the sale. He declined and, according to her account, told her that if she raised the matter publicly he would release the tapes. She left the reading room. She went home. She came back.

**Magnus Calloway** is dead before the novel's second page, but his presence structures everything that follows. He is fifty-eight, self-made, the founder of a logistics company that he sold in 2003 for an amount his biography describes as "significant" without specifying a figure — those close to him estimate three hundred million pounds. He began buying books in the 1990s with the undisciplined enthusiasm of someone who has discovered a new field and has the resources to pursue it at scale, and he matured, over two decades, into a genuinely sophisticated collector who knew what he was buying and why. He was not a scholar — he was careful to distinguish himself from scholars and to employ them rather than pretend to their status — but he was intelligent, well-briefed, and possessed of an instinct for institutional theatre that made the Archive a genuine cultural landmark rather than merely a rich man's private store. He was also, as multiple characters describe in terms that gradually converge into a portrait Strand builds with deliberate care, a man who had long understood that power over people's professional reputations was a resource he could cultivate and use. Several members of staff describe incidents, in their interviews with Mullane, that individually seem minor — an off-hand remark that made someone feel surveilled, a conversation that circled back to a previous mistake in a way that could not have been accidental — but that collectively establish a pattern of low-grade institutional coercion that Calloway maintained across his entire staff. Voss was not unique; she was simply the most consequential.

**Fenwick Crane** is a rare book dealer whose Edinburgh business, Crane & Abernethy, was destroyed by Calloway in 2015 when Calloway provided information to a major client that led to the client withdrawing from a transaction on grounds of misrepresentation — grounds that Crane maintained were fabricated, and which were investigated by the Antiquarian Booksellers' Association without conclusive finding. Crane lost the transaction, lost the client, lost a subsequent consignment that was pulled by a third party citing reputational concerns, and eventually lost the business. He was present at the auction as a guest of one of the bidders, a circumstance that he was unable to explain to Mullane's satisfaction for an uncomfortable portion of the investigation. He is the novel's most straightforwardly suspicious figure: he has motive, he was present, and he behaves under questioning with the barely suppressed fury of a man who has spent years composing what he would say to the person who destroyed him. He did not kill Calloway, but his alibi — he was drinking with the bidder he accompanied until well past midnight, in a bar that has CCTV — takes six days to verify because the bidder initially provides an account of the evening that does not quite match Crane's, a discrepancy caused entirely by the bidder's own embarrassment about how much they drank and at what point they ceased to be a reliable witness.

**Professor Alec Dunmore** is a visiting scholar from St Andrews with a long history of accusing Calloway of acquiring items of questionable provenance — specifically, a collection of sixteenth-century Scottish manuscript maps that Dunmore maintained had been removed from an estate sale in 2009 before they were properly catalogued and that should have been offered to the National Library. Calloway's legal team had seen off Dunmore's complaints twice, but Dunmore had recently published an article in a specialist journal revisiting the provenance questions with new documentary evidence, and he was at the auction at Calloway's personal invitation — an invitation that everyone who knew Calloway understood as a demonstration of contempt, a public performance of untouchability. Dunmore's presence at the Archive on the night of Calloway's death, combined with his published hostility, makes him a plausible suspect through most of the novel's first half, until Mullane establishes that he left the Archive at ten-fifteen and has door-entry records from his faculty building in St Andrews at six-twenty the following morning.

## Plot Summary

The novel opens at 6:47 on the morning of 14 February — a detail that Strand notes without emphasis and the significance of which is not immediately apparent — when Raquel Ife arrives at the Archive to find the vault's status light displaying amber, indicating the interior deadbolt has been engaged. She follows protocol: she calls Dr. Voss, she calls the operations director, she does not attempt to open the door. Voss arrives twenty minutes later and is, Ife notes, already dressed as if she had not been sleeping — in her work clothes rather than in whatever she was wearing at home, which Ife attributes at the time to Voss's usual professional standards. When the door is opened and the body found, it is Voss who establishes the immediate facts with quiet efficiency: Calloway is dead, he is seated at the reading table, the Folio is open before him, there are no signs of a struggle. She places her fingers on his neck. She pronounces the absence of a pulse. She calls the police.

Mullane arrives with her team at eight-fifty. Her first-impression notes, quoted at intervals throughout the novel, record the vault's geography, the position of the body, and three details she flags for follow-up: the Armagnac glass contains a very small residue of liquid that the glass does not appear to have been chosen from the Archive's own serving set; the First Folio is opened to the Prologue of *The Tempest*, which is not the opening of the play — someone opened it to that specific page; and the climate control panel on the vault's interior wall shows a manual override has been engaged at 11:23 the previous evening, adjusting the humidity setpoint upward by three percent for a period of ninety minutes before returning to the standard setting. None of these details has a self-evident meaning at this stage, but Mullane records them and returns to each in turn.

The post-mortem, conducted within forty-eight hours, establishes that Calloway died of cardiac arrest — not implausible for a man of fifty-eight with a known history of atrial fibrillation — but also finds trace quantities of a compound consistent with flecainide, an antiarrhythmic drug, in his bloodstream at a concentration significantly above therapeutic range. Flecainide in overdose can precipitate ventricular fibrillation in patients with certain cardiac profiles, including Calloway's. The finding transforms the investigation from a suspicious death inquiry to a murder investigation. The difficulty remains: the door was locked from the inside, and no one else was in the vault.

Mullane's investigation of the Archive's staff and records in the days following the death produces the social topography of the institution with increasing granularity. She interviews all fourteen members of staff, reviews the Archive's financial records going back five years, and obtains the guest list and security logs for the auction night. The auction had been attended by seven guests: three bidders, each with an accompanying advisor; the auctioneer, who was not a professional but Calloway's personal lawyer acting in that capacity; and Fenwick Crane as the guest of one bidder. All had left the building before eleven o'clock, confirmed by the front-door entry system. The security log shows no entries after 11:17 — when Voss's access card was used to enter the building — and no exits thereafter. If the security log is accurate, Voss entered the Archive at 11:17 and did not leave until the door was opened the following morning. Voss's account is that she returned to the building to collect a scarf she had forgotten. She was inside, she says, for less than five minutes.

The scarf is present in the building. Mullane notes this. She does not initially know whether its presence proves or disproves anything, since it could have been left there deliberately or forgotten legitimately, but she records the detail and begins to treat Voss's account of the evening with the particular form of careful attention she reserves for statements that are verifiable and verified but that do not fully account for what might have happened.

The investigation's first major public complication arrives when a crime reporter at The Scotsman obtains information — its source is never established — about the spectral analysis Voss had commissioned privately and the questions it raised about the Folio's authenticity. The story runs on the fourth day of the investigation under the headline "Death of Billionaire Archivist: Questions Raised Over £14 Million Folio." The story does not name Voss as the commissioner of the analysis, identifying her only as "a senior member of Archive staff," but within the small world of the rare books community it is immediately understood who is meant. The effect on the investigation is double: it introduces the forgery angle to public attention, and it causes Christopher Dunbar-Knox's legal team to contact Police Scotland requesting access to the evidence regarding the Folio's authenticity, a development that adds institutional pressure to an inquiry already running at high temperature.

Mullane uses the news story as an opportunity to re-interview Voss, presenting it as an information-sharing exercise rather than an interrogation. It is this interview — the long, deceptively quiet conversation in the reading room — that begins to unravel Voss's account. Mullane does not challenge her directly on the authentication; she discusses the general difficulty of manuscript authentication in the contemporary market, the ways in which commercial pressure could distort scholarly judgment, the mechanisms by which institutions managed internal disagreement. Voss, responding as a scholar and professional rather than as a suspect, says more than she should about the subjective nature of the judgment she made in 2012 and about the difficulty of her position once the acquisition had been announced publicly. She says nothing that is incriminating on its face, but the accumulation of qualifications and hedges in her account of the 2012 assessment is a document in itself, and Mullane leaves the interview with a clear picture of someone who is maintaining a story by dint of enormous effort.

The locked-room problem is not solved until the ninth day of the investigation, when Mullane's forensic team re-examines the vault's climate control documentation and discovers that the manual override at 11:23 — which initially appeared to be merely an unexplained humidity adjustment — was accompanied by a secondary command in the system log that is consistent with a forced reboot of the biometric door sensor. The reboot would have taken the biometric system offline for approximately ninety seconds, during which time the door could have been opened from outside using only the keypad code. The manual deadbolt could then have been engaged from inside, and the door closed from outside — with someone still outside — while leaving the deadbolt engaged. The deadbolt, as Mullane's team re-examines the vault door with this in mind, was not engaged from inside by a person. It was engaged by a thin flexible rod inserted through the gap between door and frame — a gap of 4.3 millimetres, wide enough for the purpose — after the door was pulled shut. The gap was visible in the initial forensic photographs but had not been identified as significant. Once Mullane knows to look for evidence of how the rod was inserted and withdrawn, her team finds two microscopic paint transfers on the door frame consistent with the rod having been retracted after engagement, leaving almost no trace but not quite none.

The mechanism explains the locked room. The flecainide explains the cause of death. What remains is the delivery method, and it is found in the Armagnac glass: the residue contains traces of a pharmaceutical-grade flecainide suspension that had been mixed into the drink before it was poured. The glass was not from the Archive's serving set because Voss brought it — brought a glass in which she had already prepared the dose, and which she left on the table after Calloway drank from it, replacing the Archive's glass she had removed. The substitution is the physical evidence Mullane was waiting for: the Archive's glasses are etched with a small thistle motif, and the glass found at the scene has no such etching. When Mullane's team canvasses Voss's flat, they find one thistle-etched glass wrapped in a cloth in a kitchen drawer that had been cleaned but not thoroughly enough.

Voss is arrested at the Archive on the eleventh day of the investigation. She does not deny the physical facts once Mullane presents them. Her account of why, given in a lengthy statement, is a precise and careful document: she describes the authentication, the pressure, the discovery of the recording, the seven years of blackmail, the auction, the request to halt the sale, and Calloway's response. She acknowledges that she planned the death from the moment she left the Archive that evening and returned home to collect the flecainide, which she obtained through her partner, a recently retired GP who kept a dispensary of samples that she had known about for years. She describes returning to the Archive at 11:17, going to the reading room where Calloway was finishing the last of the auction paperwork, telling him she had reconsidered and wanted to discuss terms, pouring the drink while his attention was on the papers, waiting while he died — which took twenty-two minutes — and then arranging the scene, engaging the deadbolt mechanism, and leaving. She says she opened the First Folio to *The Tempest* Prologue because Prospero, she thought, was the appropriate presiding spirit: a man who had made himself master of an island and everything on it, and who was nevertheless undone by the things he had tried to control.

## Mystery Elements

The central mystery of *The Glass Archivist* is constructed on two interlocking puzzles, and Strand is careful to make them interdependent: the locked-room problem cannot be fully solved without understanding the forgery, and the motive for the murder cannot be fully understood without understanding the locked-room mechanism, because the mechanism demonstrates a level of premeditation and technical preparation that points toward someone with intimate knowledge of the vault. Most killers in locked-room mysteries are shown to have exploited a physical quirk of the space by accident or after brief observation; Voss's method required her to have studied the vault's security documentation in detail, to have tested the door gap manually, and to have understood the climate control system's reboot protocol well enough to trigger it remotely from within the building. This is not the crime of a person acting in the heat of the moment. It is the crime of a person who made a deliberate decision and then executed it with professional precision — which is, Strand suggests, exactly how Voss approached everything she did.

The killer is Dr. Petra Voss, whose motive is a compound of professional self-preservation, long-accumulated rage, and what she describes in her statement as an absolute refusal to be the instrument of a fraud she could no longer rationalise. The flecainide was administered in a prepared Armagnac that she brought to the building in a bag, the glass substitution executed while Calloway's attention was elsewhere. The locked-room mechanism depended on the 4.3-millimetre gap in the vault door frame and a rod she had fashioned from the spine wire of an out-of-use archival folder — a piece of equipment she had access to in quantity and that would not be missed. The climate control reboot was triggered through the Archive's internal maintenance tablet, which she had administrator access to, creating a 90-second window during which she could operate the keypad without the biometric record being created. She entered the vault, engaged the deadbolt via the rod while standing in the doorway, pulled the door shut, and withdrew the rod. The entire mechanism was tested, she acknowledges, on two earlier evenings when she stayed late in the building alone.

The novel plants three primary red herrings, each of which is sustained for a significant portion of the narrative and each of which is constructed with enough specificity to be genuinely misleading. Fenwick Crane is the most immediately plausible suspect: he lost his business because of Calloway, he was present at the auction, and his behaviour under questioning is genuinely evasive — not because he is guilty but because his presence at the auction involved a private arrangement with the bidder he accompanied, an arrangement that involved an undisclosed commission on the sale that he was not certain was entirely legal. His alibi takes six days to verify because the bidder's initial account of their shared evening is inaccurate, and during those six days Crane's documented hostility to Calloway and his knowledge of the Archive make him a productive focus for investigative attention. Strand uses this red herring structurally: it establishes the financial and reputational ecosystem surrounding Calloway's death and ensures that the reader's attention is on the auction and its commercial context for the novel's first act, which is exactly where Strand needs it to be before she redirects it to the authentication history.

Professor Alec Dunmore is the intellectual red herring: a man whose grievance is documented, whose expertise makes him capable of understanding the value and the vulnerability of the Folio, and whose presence at the auction at Calloway's personal invitation — understood by everyone as a provocation — creates a motive structure that is superficially compelling. His alibi, the door-entry record from his faculty building in St Andrews, is solid, but Mullane does not confirm it until the fifth day of the investigation, and during that interval his account of his movements after leaving the Archive contains a gap of approximately three hours that he explains as a solitary drive during which he processed his anger about the evening. The drive cannot be verified or disproved. Strand allows Dunmore to remain genuinely ambiguous in the reader's mind through the novel's middle section, and his eventual elimination from the inquiry leaves the investigation, and the reader, in a position of reduced certainty that Strand exploits by introducing the climate control anomaly at almost exactly the same moment.

The third red herring is structural rather than individual: it is the locked-room problem itself. Strand uses the apparent impossibility of the crime scene to direct the investigation — and the reader's attention — toward solutions that require someone other than the person who left the building last. The first three days of the investigation are dominated by attempts to identify a means by which someone could have been inside the vault without appearing in the security log, and several plausible-sounding theories are advanced and dismissed: a failure in the biometric recording that might have missed an entry, a possible secondary access point in the climate control ducting, the theoretical possibility of the door having been locked through a mechanical fault. Each of these theories is pursued seriously and eliminated honestly, and their elimination appears to shrink the pool of suspects toward zero — because Voss's presence in the building is confirmed by the security log, and the investigation has proceeded on the implicit assumption that someone present in the building would not have been able to lock the vault from outside after leaving Calloway inside. The discovery of the door gap mechanism inverts this assumption, and its inversion simultaneously explains the locked room and points, with sudden clarity, toward the only person in the building at the relevant time.

The narrative's management of Voss as a character is the most technically demanding element of Strand's performance. She is present throughout — cooperative, knowledgeable, visibly distressed by Calloway's death in ways that read as authentic — and Strand gives her several moments of genuine compassion and professional clarity that are not performance but real. This is not a trick; it is a portrait. Voss does feel the loss of the Archive as an institution even as she knows what she has done to protect it. She does experience genuine grief, not for Calloway personally — she had ceased to have any feeling for him except contempt — but for the version of her professional self that she had managed to maintain across fourteen years, and that his death has made simultaneously safe and permanently compromised. Her distress in the early scenes is distress about the future of the Archive, about the fate of the collection, about whether the Folio's exposure as a forgery will taint every other item in the building. This is not misdirection; it is accurate psychology, and its accuracy is what makes Voss's final revelation feel earned rather than arbitrary.

The moment of revelation — Mullane's presentation of the physical evidence and Voss's decision not to deny it — is the most restrained such scene in contemporary British crime fiction of the past decade. There is no breakdown, no protestation of necessity, no plea for understanding. Voss listens to the evidence with the attention she would give to a scholarly argument she had not encountered before. She asks two questions: one about the paint-transfer evidence (was the composition analysis conclusive?), one about the glass (where exactly was the thistle etching placed on Archive glassware?). Both questions are technical. Both are the questions of a person verifying the quality of the case against her rather than trying to find an escape from it. When Mullane finishes, Voss says: "I see. I would like to make a statement. I will tell you everything." And she does. The statement takes four hours. At its conclusion, Voss asks whether the Archive's other holdings will be protected from the legal proceedings, and expresses, in language that Mullane describes in her case notes as "not remorseful but precise," the hope that the books themselves — the genuine ones, which she lists from memory in taxonomic order — will find a proper institutional home. The last line of the novel is Mullane's note from that conversation: "She never asked about herself."

## Themes

- **Authenticity and forgery as moral categories.** The First Folio forgery is not merely the MacGuffin around which the plot assembles; it is the novel's central metaphor for the question of authenticity that runs through every character and relationship in the book. Strand is interested in the difference between something that is genuinely what it claims to be and something that has been made to appear that way — and in the question of whether the distinction matters when the performance is sustained for long enough. The forgery is magnificent, a work of art that could pass for the genuine article under conditions that most scholars would consider rigorous. Voss's authentication is not incompetent; it is compromised. These are different failures, and Strand insists on the difference. The novel ultimately suggests that authenticity is not a property of objects but a practice of honest attention, and that the corruption of that practice — in scholarship, in professional relationships, in the self-presentation of the powerful — is a form of violence against everyone who trusted it.

- **Institutional power and the culture of silence.** Calloway's management of Voss through the implicit threat of reputational destruction is the novel's most contemporary concern, and Strand draws it with a specificity that its reviewers noted as one of the book's most striking qualities. The blackmail is never named, never transacted as an explicit exchange — there is no "you will do this or I will release the tapes." It operates through implication and atmosphere: the awareness that the recording exists, that it is in Calloway's possession, that its release would end her career, is sufficient. It creates a condition in which Voss's professional excellence — her genuine contribution to the Archive over fourteen years — becomes simultaneously the proof of her captivity and the mechanism of her survival. Strand is attentive to the way this kind of institutional coercion is routinely invisible to outside observers and even to the other staff who are subject to less extreme versions of it. Several members of the Archive staff describe, in their interviews with Mullane, incidents that individually seem like the behaviour of a demanding employer and that collectively establish a management environment in which everyone is aware of performing for a judgment whose criteria are never fully articulated.

- **The epistemology of expertise.** One of the novel's most sustained interests is in the question of how expert knowledge is produced, maintained, and potentially corrupted. Voss is an authority on early printed books in a field where authority rests on a combination of technical knowledge, professional reputation, and the kind of accumulated pattern recognition that cannot be fully articulated as a method. When she authenticates the Folio — initially, before the pressure — she is drawing on twenty-five years of examining similar objects. When she revises her assessment, she is drawing on the same knowledge base but producing a different output, and the difference between the two assessments is not in the evidence but in what she allows herself to conclude from it. Strand uses this to explore a more general point about expertise: that it is not a purely cognitive achievement but a social and institutional one, dependent on conditions that include professional security, freedom from coercive pressure, and the willingness to give unwelcome answers to powerful people who would prefer a different conclusion.

- **The archaeology of professional complicity.** Voss has been complicit in the forgery's presence in the Archive for seven years by the time she kills Calloway, and this complicity has accreted layer by layer — the initial equivocation, the revised report, the silence about the doubts she retained, the spectral analysis she finally commissions but holds privately rather than making public. At each stage, a smaller act of honesty would have prevented the next stage from becoming necessary. Strand presents this not as a simple story of escalating moral failure but as a study in the way that small professional compromises create structural conditions that make subsequent large compromises feel, from inside them, like the only available option. Voss's decision to kill Calloway is the most extreme term in a sequence that begins with a sentence in a 2012 report that did not say quite what she believed, and Strand shows the whole sequence without ellipsis — including the moments at which different choices were genuinely available but not taken.

- **The love of objects and what it costs.** Voss loves the books. This is not a minor or decorative detail; it is central to the novel's moral structure and to the specific tragedy of Voss's position. She has spent her professional life in the service of objects whose value is partly monetary, partly scholarly, and partly something harder to articulate — a quality of surviving witness, of being a physical artefact of the moment of their creation that no digital reproduction can replicate. Her care for the Archive's genuine holdings is authentic and unperformed. The forgery offends her not just because it is technically inaccurate but because it occupies the place of a real thing — it takes up the cultural and emotional space that a genuine First Folio would deserve and fills it with an imposture that, if perpetuated, poisons the Archive's relationship with everything real it contains. This is why she cannot allow the sale to proceed: not primarily because it will expose her, but because it will extend the imposture indefinitely and bind the Archive to a lie that will eventually corrode everything she has genuinely built there. The love of objects is what put her in the position she is in, and it is also what finally made inaction impossible.

- **Justice, accountability, and the limits of the law.** Strand resists the genre convention of closing the novel at the point of arrest. The final chapter takes place six months after Voss's conviction — she pleads guilty to culpable homicide, citing diminished responsibility related to the sustained psychological coercion she had been subject to, and receives a seven-year sentence — and it follows Mullane on a visit to the Archive, which has been transferred to the stewardship of the National Library of Scotland following lengthy legal proceedings. The Folio has been retained as a museum piece — not exhibited as a First Folio but displayed as an example of exceptional twentieth-century forgery craftsmanship. A small card beside it reads: "The maker of this volume is unknown. It was purchased as an authentic First Folio in 2012 for fourteen million pounds. The purchase price was refunded." Mullane stands in front of it for a long time. Strand does not tell us what she thinks. The novel closes on this image: a magnificent fake, correctly labelled at last, in a room that now houses genuine things.
