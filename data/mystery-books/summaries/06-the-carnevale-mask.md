# The Carnevale Mask

**Author:** Gianfranco Levi
**Published:** 1987
**Genre:** Mystery — Historical Noir

---

## Overview

Gianfranco Levi's *The Carnevale Mask* — first published in Italian as *La Maschera del Carnevale* and translated into English by Marion Ashworth in 1991 — is widely considered the masterwork of an author who spent his career writing fiction at the intersection of postwar Italian memory and the formal mechanics of the detective novel. Levi, who was born in Venice in 1931, was seven years old when Mussolini's racial laws came into effect and twelve when the German occupation began. He survived the war in circumstances he never fully described publicly, and this reticence — this refusal to render his own experience as testimony — permeates his fiction in ways that make it richer rather than thinner: the war in his novels is never the set piece but always the undertow, always the thing pulling at the legs of characters who are trying to walk forward in their lives.

The novel is set over the final forty-eight hours of Carnevale in Venice, in a year Levi fixes as 1964 without being precise about the month — though the mathematics of liturgical calculation in that year places the scene in late February. It is narrated in close third person by Commissario Ettore Valli of the Venice questura, a man of fifty-two who has survived the war by a combination of luck and moral compromise that he has spent twenty years failing to come to terms with. Valli is assigned to the case when the body of Renato Sanzio, one of the most celebrated Italian filmmakers of the postwar renaissance, is discovered floating in a side canal off the Campo San Polo in the early hours of the last night of Carnevale. Sanzio is wearing a Bauta mask that belongs to someone else — a mask that was supposed to be worn by a woman named Giulia Foscari, who was last seen at the party at which Sanzio also appeared, and who is now nowhere to be found.

The novel operates simultaneously as a precise mystery plot and as a meditation on the moral architecture of postwar Italian society — on how a nation that collaborated, to greater or lesser degrees, with fascism rebuilt itself as a nation of anti-fascists, and what that reconstruction cost in terms of truth. Levi's particular genius is his ability to make this argument not through polemic or even explicit thematic statement but through the mechanics of his plot, in which the murder turns out to be an act of self-preservation by a man whose postwar identity was built on the suppression of documented evidence of his wartime crimes. The mystery, in other words, is a mystery about memory — about what was hidden, and why, and what it costs when the hidden thing resurfaces.

*The Carnevale Mask* won the Premio Scerbanenco for Italian crime fiction in the year of its publication and was translated into fourteen languages. The English translation received the Crime Writers' Association International Dagger in 1992. It is regularly cited by Italian critics as one of the essential documents of a particular postwar Italian experience that has no clean memorial: the experience of people who did not resist, who survived, and who built the republic on foundations they preferred not to examine.

## Setting

Venice during Carnevale is a city that has temporarily agreed to be someone else, and Levi uses this collective performance as the central metaphor of his novel. The Carnevale of the 1960s was not yet the mass tourist spectacle it would become — it had been officially abolished by Mussolini in the 1930s and had only recently been informally revived as a social practice among the Venetian upper classes and the artistic community. The celebrations in the novel take place in private palazzos and small campo gatherings, in the better restaurants and in the streets of the more prosperous sestieri, among people who know each other and are pretending, for a night or two, that they don't. The mask is the operative symbol: a tradition whose original function was the suspension of class distinctions, allowing the nobleman and the commoner to occupy the same social space, has been recuperated as an entertainment — but the metaphor of concealment, of the self that operates behind the face presented to the world, remains fully available to Levi's purposes.

The physical Venice of the novel is the Venice of the early 1960s, before the mass tourism of later decades had fully imposed its aesthetic transformation on the city. Levi describes it with the authority of someone who grew up within its particular geography: the fog that rolls in off the lagoon in late February, thick enough to muffle the sound of footsteps on wet stone; the smell of canal water in winter, which is cold and slightly stagnant and nothing like the romantic vision; the narrowness of the calli through which two people can barely pass; the sudden openings of campo and campiello that surprise you even when you have walked the route a hundred times. The questura where Valli works is in the Ca' Foscari district, a building of faded bureaucratic grandeur smelling of damp paper and pine disinfectant. The canal where Sanzio's body is found is a minor waterway off the Campo San Polo — not picturesque, not famous, the kind of canal that appears on no tourist map.

The Palazzo Vestri, where the party at which both Sanzio and Giulia Foscari were last seen takes place, is rendered in particular detail. It is a fifteenth-century palazzo on the Grand Canal, the home of Aldo Vestri and his wife Camilla, and its public rooms are furnished with the kind of cultivated elegance that announces money without shouting it — second-rate Venetian masters on the walls, correct but not ostentatious glassware, a terrazzo floor that has been polished so many times it shows no longer the marks of its original laying. Vestri has spent thirty years constructing around himself an environment that speaks of continuity, of established cultural legitimacy, of a family and a life that have always been more or less as they are now. The palazzo is the physical expression of this construction. Levi returns to it repeatedly in the investigation, and with each return it seems subtly different — more defensive, more anxiously maintained, more clearly a stage set rather than a home.

The temporal setting of late Carnevale — the final night, when the celebrations reach their peak before Ash Wednesday's ceremonial close — adds a layer of structural irony that Levi exploits fully. In the Christian tradition, Carnevale is the last feast before Lent, the last night of permitted excess before the season of penance and self-examination begins. Sanzio's death on this night, at the hands of a man who has spent nineteen years avoiding penance and self-examination for genuine crimes, creates a thematic resonance that the novel earns rather than merely asserts. The morning after Carnevale, when Valli conducts his interviews in the cold grey light of the first day of Lent, carries the weight of that tradition without the novel ever naming it directly.

## Main Characters

### Commissario Ettore Valli

Ettore Valli has been with the Venice questura for seventeen years, having joined the police force after the liberation with the modest ambition of being useful in whatever way was available to a man of forty-one with no particular skills except a talent for listening carefully and an ability to make people feel that their words were valued. He is not a brilliant detective in the tradition of the classical genre; he is a meticulous and patient one, a man who succeeds through accumulation rather than inspiration, who is more interested in understanding why things happened than in the procedural triumph of establishing how. His colleagues find him reliable but slightly unnerving — there is something in his manner that suggests he is always looking for something that isn't on the surface of events, and this is correct.

Valli's wartime history is disclosed gradually and obliquely through the novel. He was a low-level functionary in the municipal bureaucracy of the German occupation period — not a collaborator in any dramatic sense, but a man who kept his head down, did what was required of him, and survived while others did not. His wife, Antonia, was Jewish; she survived the war hidden in a convent in the Veneto, a fact that Valli presents to himself as evidence that he did what was important when it mattered, evidence he has been interrogating without resolution for twenty years. His understanding of the moral landscape he is walking through in this investigation — a landscape in which the question of what people did during the war and what they have made of themselves since sits beneath every conversation like deep water beneath a thin sheet of ice — is intimate and specific in a way that makes him uniquely qualified to solve it. It also makes him uniquely reluctant to arrive at certain conclusions.

He is a widower; Antonia died in 1958 of a heart condition, and he has not remarried. He lives alone in a three-room apartment in the Dorsoduro, keeps a cat named Ercole, and spends his evenings reading history. He is precise, slightly formal in his manner, possessed of a dry wit that he deploys infrequently and with great accuracy. He has never in his career arrested someone and felt entirely good about it. He will not feel entirely good about the arrest at the end of this novel either, and Levi is honest about why: to arrest Aldo Vestri for the murder of Renato Sanzio is to win a justice that is true but incomplete, to resolve a crime while leaving unresolved the larger crimes that produced it — the collaboration, the denunciations, the deals struck between ambitious men and a murderous regime that killed people whose names are now in no record Vestri ever acknowledged.

### Renato Sanzio

Renato Sanzio, the victim, was sixty-one years old at the time of his death and at the height of a career that had survived the transition from neorealism to the more formally experimental Italian cinema of the 1960s through what his admirers called adaptability and his critics called opportunism. He had made his first feature in 1948, a rigorously observed story of postwar poverty in the Veneto that established him as a significant figure in Italian cinema, and had subsequently made eleven more films of varying ambition and achievement. His most recent, *I Testimoni Silenziosi* (The Silent Witnesses), released in 1962, was a formally ambitious exploration of Italian memory and complicity that had divided critics between those who found it a masterwork and those who found it a self-serving piece of mystification by a director comfortable enough in the culture of forgetting that he could make art about it without threatening anyone who mattered.

In the world of the novel, Sanzio is a figure of genuine prestige and genuine complexity — a man whose artistic ambitions were real, whose intelligence was considerable, and whose private behavior was often at variance with his public persona as a committed moralist. He had a long-running and increasingly bitter professional relationship with Aldo Vestri that had begun as a partnership of genuine shared vision and had curdled, over thirty years, into mutual economic dependency and private contempt. He had a history of romantic relationships with younger women, marked by a pattern of initial idealization and subsequent manipulation that left several of his former companions in states of varying damage. He was, in sum, a man whom it was easy to admire from a distance and difficult to respect up close, and his death leaves behind a complex of genuine grief, genuine relief, and a very large number of people with something they would prefer not to have examined.

The fact that emerges slowly in Valli's investigation — the fact that is the true motor of the novel's plot — is that Sanzio had spent the last three years of his life accumulating documentary evidence of Vestri's collaboration with the Fascist regime and the German occupation. He had found, through a combination of archival research and the testimony of elderly survivors in the Veneto, footage that Vestri had shot professionally during the occupation — propaganda material, record footage, and, most damning of all, footage of roundups that Vestri had filmed for the German authorities in exchange for protection and continued access to film stock. Sanzio had not yet decided what to do with this material, and the novel suggests that his indecision was not merely strategic but genuinely moral — he was not a good enough man to be certain he was doing this for the right reasons.

### Aldo Vestri

Aldo Vestri is sixty-three years old, wealthy, publicly respected, and living in a state of sustained existential terror that has become so habitual he no longer experiences it as fear but simply as the cost of being alive. He came from a modest family in the Veneto, trained as a cameraman, worked his way into the film industry in Rome in the late 1930s, and made the series of decisions during the German occupation that he has spent the subsequent nineteen years making himself forget. The decisions were not trivial; they involved the filming of material that was used in the identification of Jewish families in the Veneto, material whose production Vestri has told himself — and perhaps even occasionally believes — was forced upon him, unavoidable, the price of survival for himself and his family.

He built his postwar career with exceptional industry and intelligence. The production company he founded in Rome in 1947 became one of the major forces in Italian cinema of the 1950s, producing films with social and moral ambition — including Sanzio's early work — that positioned Vestri as a man of the left, a man whose aesthetic commitments were inseparable from his political ones, a man who had suffered under fascism and emerged with his conscience intact. This identity is not entirely false; Vestri did suffer, did resist in certain ways, did produce work of genuine value. The identity is, however, built on a foundation that would not survive the production of Sanzio's footage, and Vestri knows it with the certainty of a man who has examined the foundations constantly for nineteen years looking for a crack he might be able to repair.

What makes Vestri terrifying as a character is the clarity of his self-knowledge. He knows exactly what he is protecting and exactly what it cost him to protect it. He is not self-deceived; he is self-disciplined, which is more dangerous. When he killed Sanzio — on the stairs of the Palazzo Vestri in the final hour of Carnevale, using a heavy Murano glass candlestick that he subsequently cleaned and returned to its place on the mantelpiece — he did so with full consciousness of what he was doing and why. He has been preparing for the possibility that someone would find the footage for years, rehearsing responses, maintaining relationships with people in positions to help him manage such a crisis. What he had not accounted for was Sanzio's decision to bring the evidence to Carnevale physically, in an envelope tucked inside his costume, with the evident intention of delivering it somewhere that night.

### Giulia Foscari

Giulia Foscari, twenty-eight years old, is the daughter of a Venetian family of modest means whose primary social capital is the considerable beauty of its daughters and the marriage connections they can make with it. She appears initially as the novel's most urgent puzzle — the woman whose mask is on the dead man's face, who was last seen leaving the Vestri party with someone she presumably trusted, who is now missing from her lodgings and unreachable by telephone. The question of whether she is a second victim or a witness in flight drives much of the novel's first half.

In fact, she is neither. Giulia Foscari was Sanzio's current companion — a relationship that her family approved of, given Sanzio's wealth and connections, and that she herself had complex feelings about, given Sanzio's age and the pattern she had begun to recognize in his behavior toward her. She was present at the Vestri party as Sanzio's guest, and she witnessed, without fully understanding, the argument between Sanzio and Vestri that preceded the murder. She fled the party not because she saw the murder but because she saw Vestri's face in the aftermath — before the body was discovered, in the moment when he returned to the party and she read in his expression something that she did not want to read — and her flight was not premeditated but instinctive, the response of someone who understood that she knew something dangerous without knowing exactly what it was.

She is found on the morning of Ash Wednesday in the apartment of her cousin in Mestre, across the lagoon, where she has been for thirty-six hours, unable to explain even to herself exactly what she is afraid of or why. Valli's interview with her is one of the novel's finest scenes — a patient, gentle excavation of a testimony that the witness does not know she has, a conversation in which Valli leads her through the events of the previous night in such careful sequence that she arrives, almost without realizing it, at the precise description of Vestri's expression in the moment that will become the pivot of the case.

## Plot Summary

The novel opens in the small hours of the last morning of Carnevale. A boatman named Alvise Zucchini, making his pre-dawn passage along the rio off Campo San Polo, spots what he initially takes to be a Carnevale dummy floating against the canal wall — a figure in a black Bauta cloak and tricorn hat, face-down in the water — and realizes, as he poles closer, that it is not a dummy. He pulls the figure to the bank and raises the alarm, and the first patrol officer on the scene confirms that the man in the water is wearing a Bauta mask of a particular, unusual type: a mask of pale ivory silk decorated with silver thread in a pattern of water lilies, a mask that is clearly not a cheap Carnevale purchase but a commissioned piece of some expense. The mask is remembered by the officer's sergeant, who attended the same party the previous evening: it was the mask worn at the Vestri party by Giulia Foscari, daughter of the Foscari family, companion of Renato Sanzio.

Valli is called to the scene at six in the morning and makes his preliminary examination in the cold grey light of the canal bank. The body is identified quickly: Renato Sanzio, filmmaker, sixty-one, dressed in a black Carnevale costume under which he wore evening clothes. The cause of death, pending the medical examiner's determination, appears to be blunt trauma to the back of the skull, with the canal death secondary — he was struck before he entered the water, not drowned. The mask on his face is wrong: his own mask, a traditional Bauta of white plaster, is found floating ten meters away. Someone replaced it with Giulia Foscari's mask, either before or after he entered the water. Giulia Foscari herself is absent from her lodgings and has not been seen since she left the Vestri party sometime after midnight.

Valli conducts his first round of interviews that morning, while the city performs its ash-marked transition from excess to austerity. He speaks to Aldo Vestri at the Palazzo Vestri at nine a.m., finding a man who presents himself as shocked and grief-stricken in precisely the manner of someone who has been preparing to appear shocked and grief-stricken. Vestri's account of the previous evening is detailed, coherent, and contains one element that Valli notes silently: Vestri describes seeing Sanzio at the party as late as one in the morning, which would mean Sanzio left the party no more than an hour before his body was found, which would mean the murder took place very close to the palazzo, if not inside it.

The second interview, with Claudia Menci, the actress who had a celebrated and extremely public breakup with Sanzio the previous year, takes place at her apartment in the Dorsoduro. Menci is imperious, sardonic, and possessed of an alibi for the entire evening — she attended a different Carnevale party in the Cannaregio, photographed extensively throughout the night by two newspaper photographers who were there to cover the event — but she provides Valli with a piece of information that interests him: that Sanzio had recently been doing research that he described to her as "archaeology," and that she had understood this to mean archival work of some kind, connected to a film project he had mentioned without specifics. She also tells him, with a directness that suggests she is not trying to frame anyone so much as refuse to protect anyone, that Sanzio's relationship with Vestri had recently become actively hostile — that at a dinner party she attended six weeks ago, Vestri had spoken about Sanzio in terms that, while superficially contemptuous, contained an undertone of genuine alarm.

The third significant interview is with Marco Ferrante, a young director of thirty-one whose second feature Sanzio had publicly condemned at the Venice Film Festival the previous September in terms that had effectively damaged its commercial prospects. Ferrante has motive — his film lost its distribution deal in the aftermath of Sanzio's criticism, a development he blames directly on Sanzio's intervention — and he has a Carnevale costume that was seen near Campo San Polo in the early hours of the morning. He is aggressive, frightened, and transparently guilty of something, which Valli eventually determines is not murder but the theft of a ring from Sanzio's finger at the scene — Ferrante happened upon the body before the alarm was raised, panicked, and took the ring (a distinctive gold piece that Sanzio wore to every public appearance) before fleeing. His confession of this lesser crime is both the most dramatic scene in the novel's second act and the most important piece of misdirection, arriving at a moment when the investigation is converging on him as the most plausible suspect.

The investigation turns on the discovery of the envelope. Sanzio's costume is examined in detail two days into the investigation, and inside a sewn pocket in the lining of his cloak, Valli's sergeant finds a sealed envelope containing a handwritten note and a list of dates, names, and locations. The note is addressed to a journalist named Cesare Arnone at the weekly magazine *L'Osservatore Civile*, and it reads: *"The footage exists. It is in the possession of the person described in the attached document. I have seen it. The truth is that this man filmed the roundup of the Mira families in November 1943 at the request of the German authorities. He was paid in film stock and equipment. The documentation is complete. I am bringing this to you tonight."* The attached document is a description, without name, of Aldo Vestri.

Valli takes forty-eight hours to confirm the identification — he is a careful man, and he understands what he is setting in motion — during which time the investigation has publicly moved toward Marco Ferrante as its primary suspect, following the revelation of his presence at the scene and his theft of the ring. The moment when Valli presents his findings to the chief queststore and the case pivots is the novel's central scene: a long, quiet conversation in a closed office in which Valli explains not just who killed Renato Sanzio but why the investigation did not get there faster, and what that slowness reflects about the institutional culture in which both men have spent their careers.

The arrest of Aldo Vestri occurs on a Thursday afternoon in early March, four days after Carnevale. Valli goes to the Palazzo Vestri alone, which is against procedure, and which his superiors will formally reprimand him for. He goes alone because he does not trust the scene to remain undistorted if anyone else is present — he is aware of the connections that exist between Vestri and certain officials, connections that have been protecting Vestri's secrets for nearly twenty years — and because he owes Sanzio, whom he did not like and did not admire and who was nevertheless right about something important, the dignity of being arrested by someone who understands the full weight of what is being done. The arrest itself is quiet. Vestri does not resist and does not deny. He asks only that Lorraine be allowed to remain in the palazzo until his daughters can come, and Valli grants this without being asked twice.

Giulia Foscari, found in Mestre the morning after the murder, becomes the prosecution's key witness. The envelope in Sanzio's cloak, recovered intact, constitutes the material evidence. The footage itself — which Vestri claimed to have destroyed but which Sanzio had, with characteristic caution, had transferred to a second copy held by his archive at the Cineteca Nazionale — is authenticated and placed in the record. Aldo Vestri is tried for murder and convicted in 1965, sentenced to twenty-two years, of which he serves nine before dying of a cardiac arrest in the prison at Padua in 1974. He never publicly acknowledges the wartime activities that the trial establishes as his motive.

## Mystery Elements

The central mystery of *The Carnevale Mask* operates on two levels simultaneously, and Levi's achievement is to make both levels feel essential rather than redundant. On the surface level, the mystery is classical: a man is found dead in a canal, wearing someone else's mask, and the question is who killed him and why. On the deeper level, the mystery is historical: the motive for the murder is itself a secret that the novel is trying to uncover, a secret that belongs not just to Aldo Vestri but to the society that made it possible for a man like Vestri to construct a new identity on the ruins of his wartime choices and to defend that identity, ultimately, with murder.

The actual killer is Aldo Vestri, Sanzio's producer and thirty-year business partner. Vestri killed Sanzio in the library of the Palazzo Vestri in the final hours of Carnevale — while the party continued in the rooms below and the sounds of the last night rose from the canal outside — using a Murano glass candlestick from the library mantelpiece. The killing was not premeditated in the sense of having been planned in advance, but it was not spontaneous either: Vestri had known for some months that Sanzio possessed damaging footage and had been considering how to respond. The immediate trigger was an encounter at the party in which Sanzio, apparently emboldened by champagne or by some private decision he had reached, told Vestri directly that he intended to deliver documentation of Vestri's wartime collaboration to the journalist Cesare Arnone before morning. The specificity of this threat — tonight, this journalist, this documentation — transformed Vestri's months of anxious consideration into a single moment of murderous clarity.

The mask exchange is the novel's central puzzle and its most elegant structural device. Vestri placed Giulia Foscari's mask on Sanzio's face after the murder and before moving the body to the canal — an action that was not clearly purposeful even to Vestri himself, as he admits under questioning. He took the mask from the coat rack where Giulia had placed it on arriving at the party (she had removed it during the evening, finding it uncomfortable) and put it on Sanzio's face in a state of extreme agitation, perhaps because the blank white of Sanzio's own plaster mask was unendurable to him. The effect — the mask on the wrong face, the missing woman — created a misdirection that Vestri did not plan but was happy to benefit from: it made the immediate question "where is Giulia Foscari" rather than "who killed Renato Sanzio," and it gave Vestri a window of time in which the investigation's initial direction was away from him.

The novel plants three substantial red herrings, each of which is carefully calibrated to a different register of plausibility. Claudia Menci, the actress, is the least structurally convincing but the most emotionally compelling: she hated Sanzio, she is intelligent and capable, and her public history with him gives her a motive legible to anyone who has followed the Italian cultural press. Levi uses her to explore the way personal animosity can masquerade as evidence, and the way investigators — and readers — are predisposed to find guilt in people who display anger rather than those who display composure. Menci's alibi is airtight, but more importantly, Menci herself is incapable of the specific kind of performance that Vestri sustains throughout the investigation: she is a woman who shows what she feels, and her open contempt for Sanzio is precisely the quality that proves she did not kill him.

Marco Ferrante, the young director, is the novel's most dangerous misdirection because his guilt is material rather than merely emotional — he was physically present at the scene, he is documented as being there, and his theft of the ring gives him a reason to lie that looks indistinguishable from the lying of someone trying to conceal murder. Levi plays this element with considerable craft, allowing Ferrante's evasiveness to accumulate into something that looks very much like a case through the middle section of the novel. The revelation that Ferrante stole a ring rather than committed a murder is deployed at the precise moment when the reader has nearly convicted him, and its effect is not just to exonerate Ferrante but to reset the reader's assumptions about what presence at a crime scene means.

The missing Giulia Foscari generates the investigation's most urgent emotional energy and its most effective temporal misdirection. Because she is missing and her mask is on the dead man's face, the first instinct of both Valli and the reader is to consider her a second victim — and the possibility that Sanzio's killer also took Giulia keeps the investigation simultaneously moving in two directions, exhausting attention. Her husband, Arturo Foscari, who is not her husband in the novel but her jealous older brother (a mistranslation in the original English edition was corrected in the 2003 revised translation), provides a textbook jealousy motive that is explored in some detail before being set aside. The revelation that Giulia is alive and hiding, and the subsequent interview in which she describes Vestri's expression, is the novel's structural pivot — the moment where the investigation changes direction entirely.

The moment of revelation is constructed in two movements. The first is private: Valli reading Sanzio's note in his office on a Tuesday morning, understanding immediately what it means and sitting for a long time before he picks up the telephone. This scene is rendered without drama or theatrical revelation — no sudden illumination, no cry of triumph. Valli reads the note, sets it down, looks out the window at the grey water of the Grand Canal, and thinks about what it means that a man spent nineteen years building something on a lie and then killed to protect it, and whether the lie and the murder are continuous or discrete. This is the kind of moral question that the classical detective novel typically refuses, and Levi's willingness to sit with it is what makes the revelation feel genuinely weighty.

The second movement is retrospective and textual: the final chapter of the novel is a quiet tour through the events of the previous days in which Valli explicitly maps the recontextualization that Vestri's guilt produces. Every scene in which Vestri appeared as the bereaved partner, the cooperative witness, the pillar of the community, is revealed in retrospect as a performance by a man who murdered his oldest professional associate and then had to play grief at a party while the body was still warm and the investigation was beginning in the canal outside. The mask — the literal mask that Vestri placed on Sanzio's face in a moment of post-murder agitation — becomes in this retrospective reading the image of everything Vestri had done for nineteen years: covering one face with another, making the dead wear the identity of the living, maintaining a surface over an abyss.

## Themes

- **Memory, identity, and the construction of the postwar self.** The novel's deepest concern is with the social contract by which postwar Italian society agreed not to look too carefully at certain wartime histories in exchange for the stability and productivity of the reconstruction. Aldo Vestri is not an anomaly in Levi's telling; he is an example — a particularly extreme case of something widespread, the construction of a new identity on the suppression of documentary truth. The murder is the moment when the cost of that construction is made literal: when what was suppressed refuses to stay suppressed, and the response to its resurgence is not confession but killing. Levi treats this not as an individual moral failure but as a social one — the conditions that allowed Vestri to build his postwar life are the same conditions that made his murder of Sanzio, in a twisted sense, rational.

- **The uses and abuses of art.** Renato Sanzio and Aldo Vestri have spent their careers making films that claim to engage honestly with Italian reality, and the novel reveals both men's artistic ambitions as inseparable from their self-interest in ways that complicate the claim of art to moral authority. Sanzio's decision to use his discovery of Vestri's footage as material for an act of exposure — rather than reporting it through official channels or simply publishing the evidence — reflects the filmmaker's instinct to aestheticize and control, which is admirable and troubling in equal measure. Vestri's postwar films, made with genuine craft and genuine political engagement, are compromised by the secret that funded his career, without being entirely reducible to that compromise. Levi refuses the clean moral accounting that would make the art either entirely tainted or entirely separable from its author's crimes.

- **The mask as existential condition.** The Bauta mask of the novel's title is the specific form of the Venetian Carnevale mask that most completely obscures the wearer's identity — covering not just the face but the lower jaw, distorting the voice, making gender as well as identity ambiguous. Levi uses this specific tradition to explore the way identity in the novel is always a performance, always a mask — that Vestri's postwar identity is a mask, that Valli's professional neutrality is a mask, that Giulia Foscari's performance of the compliant companion is a mask, and that the Carnevale tradition of collective mask-wearing is, in the novel, not an escape from ordinary social performance but a temporary acknowledgment of it. The placing of the wrong mask on the dead man's face is the image that crystallizes all of this: identity displaced, the dead wearing someone else's face, the living hiding behind theirs.

- **Justice and its limits.** Valli arrests Aldo Vestri and the prosecution succeeds, and neither of these outcomes feels entirely like justice in Levi's telling. The murder is resolved; the wartime collaboration that motivated it is documented and entered into the record; but the families of the people filmed in the roundup of November 1943 are not part of the trial, their losses are not the subject of any formal acknowledgment, and the social conditions that protected Vestri for nineteen years are not dismantled by his conviction. Levi is too honest to pretend that the resolution of a criminal case constitutes justice in any complete sense, and Valli's final reflections in the novel make clear that he understands this — that the correct and satisfying thing he has done is also, in a larger frame, inadequate.

- **The relationship between witness and perpetrator.** The novel's title — which refers simultaneously to the specific mask found on Sanzio's face, the Carnevale tradition, Vestri's postwar identity, and the documentary footage that Sanzio possessed — invokes the question of the witness: what it means to see, to record, to testify. Sanzio was killed because he was about to become a witness — to use what he knew to speak to the public record. Vestri's wartime crime was precisely the perversion of the witness function: he used his camera not to record truth for posterity but to produce evidence of a crime for the immediate use of its perpetrators. Giulia Foscari becomes, in the end, the novel's most important witness — not because she saw the murder but because she saw Vestri's face, which is to say she saw what he really was for one unguarded moment, and she refused to unsee it even when seeing it was dangerous. In Levi's moral universe, the refusal to unsee is itself a form of courage, and perhaps the only form of justice that the individual witness can offer.
