# The Oleander Garden

**Author:** Vesna Marković
**Published:** 2024
**Genre:** Mystery — Closed-circle literary thriller

---

## Overview

*The Oleander Garden* is a sun-soaked, suffocating novel of literary jealousy, stolen legacy, and the slow poison of secrets too long kept. Vesna Marković, herself a Croatian author of considerable repute, brings to the page a story that reads at once as a love letter to and an indictment of the literary world — its hierarchies, its gatekeepers, its willingness to canonize the undeserving and forget the truly gifted. The novel belongs to a grand tradition of the island mystery, that closed and claustrophobic subgenre in which geography itself becomes a trap, but Marković distinguishes her work by insisting that the most treacherous landscape is not the sheer cliffs or the azure sea but the interior world of reputation and authorship.

The novel centers on the death of Irena Vukić-Horvatinčić, one of the most celebrated — and most reclusive — literary figures in the Croatian-speaking world. Irena is a woman who made her name thirty years ago with a single extraordinary novel, *The Salt House*, and has spent the decades since refusing to publish anything further, granting exactly no interviews, and retreating to the island of Šipan off the Dalmatian coast, which she owns outright. When she summons a small circle of six people to Šipan, the invitations arrive with characteristic Irena drama: handwritten on cream paper, stamped with a personal seal, offering only the date of arrival and the instruction to bring nothing of professional significance. It is the kind of summons one does not refuse. It is, as several characters note upon arrival, also the kind of summons that carries a faint undercurrent of threat.

Marković wrote the novel over the course of four years, conducting extensive research into the Croatian literary scene of the 1980s, the underground manuscript culture that flourished during the late Yugoslav period, and the specific, grinding poverty that consumed so many writers who worked outside official channels. Her genius in *The Oleander Garden* is not merely the construction of an elegant mystery but the way she uses the mystery form to mount a serious argument about who gets to own a story, who gets remembered, and what happens to the truth when the person most invested in burying it is also the person most celebrated for telling it. The result is a novel that is simultaneously a page-turning whodunit and a quietly devastating reckoning with the ethics of literary inheritance.

## Setting

Šipan is the largest of the Elaphiti Islands, a small archipelago in the Adriatic that lies roughly twenty kilometres northwest of Dubrovnik. Marković chose it with care. The island is long-inhabited — there are Roman ruins along the western shore, Renaissance summer villas built by Ragusan nobility, stone churches whose bells carry over the water on still mornings. It is beautiful in a way that feels ancient and indifferent, the beauty of a place that has watched many generations of human drama with complete geological unconcern. It is also, critically, an island: reachable only by ferry or private boat, subject to the sudden Adriatic storms that can strand visitors for days at a time, possessed of that particular isolation that belongs to places where the mainland is visible on a clear day but might as well be on the other side of the world when the weather turns.

Irena's estate, known locally as the Vukić house though she formally renamed it Omorika after a species of Serbian spruce, occupies the southeastern tip of the island on a promontory above the sea. The main house is a converted Renaissance villa — stone walls a metre thick, terracotta floors worn to a warm gloss by centuries of use, high narrow windows that frame the Adriatic like paintings. Irena has decorated it with disciplined austerity: no books on display (a deliberate provocation, her guests all note), plain whitewashed walls, furniture that is old but not precious. The estate also includes two smaller stone outbuildings used as guest quarters, a kitchen garden maintained by the island's caretaker Josip, and the oleander garden itself, a formal enclosure on the eastern side of the property where Irena had planted dozens of oleander bushes over the years, their pink and white flowers brilliant against the stone walls.

The oleander garden deserves particular attention as a setting because Marković returns to it constantly, using it to carry thematic freight that accumulates across the novel. Oleander — *Nerium oleander* — is one of the most beautiful and most poisonous plants in the Mediterranean world. Every part of it is toxic. Ancient sailors knew to avoid burning it as firewood. Honey made from its nectar has killed people. Marković is not subtle about the symbolism, but she is precise: she does not allow the oleander to become a lazy metaphor. Instead she renders the garden with botanical specificity — the particular pink of the cultivar Irena favored, called 'Petite Salmon'; the way the flowers hold heat long after sunset; the smell, which is not quite sweet and not quite chemical but something in between — so that by the time a body is found there, the garden has become a fully realized place rather than a symbol, and the symbolism deepens precisely because the place is so real.

The novel's sense of place extends beyond the physical island into its temporal setting. The story unfolds over four days in late September, when the summer tourists have largely gone and the island returns to its off-season self: quieter, more watchful, the light gone golden and slanted, the sea a deeper blue than July. September in the Adriatic carries a particular melancholy, a sense of things ending, and Marković deploys it deliberately. There are also extended flashback sequences set in Zagreb in 1985 and 1987, and these feel like a different world entirely — the grey, constrained world of late socialist Yugoslavia, where a young writer named Branko Tomljanović worked as a postal clerk by day and wrote his extraordinary novel by night, in longhand, in a rented room in the Trnje district that smelled of damp and coal smoke. The contrast between the lush private island in the present and the cramped, anonymous poverty of Zagreb's past is one of the novel's most devastating achievements.

## Main Characters

**Irena Vukić-Horvatinčić** is the novel's dead woman, its organizing absence, the figure around whom every other character orients themselves. She appears in the novel's first chapter, alive and imperious, conducting a dinner on the terrace with the composed authority of someone who has spent decades commanding attention. She is sixty-seven years old, lean, silver-haired, possessed of a precise and slightly terrifying intelligence that she deploys like a scalpel. Her one novel, *The Salt House*, published in 1991, won every prize of significance in Croatia, was translated into twenty-two languages, and was acclaimed internationally as a masterwork of post-war Central European literature — a novel about memory, displacement, and the experience of a Dalmatian family across two generations that managed to be both intimately local and universally human. What none of her admirers knew, and what Irena had spent thirty years protecting, was that *The Salt House* was not hers. She had stolen it — taken it whole from an unpublished manuscript written by a young Zagreb writer, made small changes in language and structure, and published it under her name while its author died in poverty, unknown. Marković renders Irena with a complexity that refuses both simple condemnation and false sympathy. She is genuinely brilliant, genuinely cruel, and genuinely, in the final months of her life, genuinely consumed by something that may or may not be conscience.

**Thomas Fell** is a British literary biographer in his early fifties, the author of three well-regarded critical studies including *The Architecture of Absence: The Life and Work of Irena Vukić-Horvatinčić*, which had been his most successful book and had essentially defined the international critical reception of Irena's work. Fell is physically unassuming — mild-featured, slightly overweight, dressed in the deliberate informality of the academic who wants to be taken for a gentleman of leisure — but his intelligence is sharp and his ambitions are sharper. He had been cooperating with Irena on what was intended to be a definitive expanded biography, visiting Šipan several times over the previous two years, going through her papers, conducting recorded interviews. He presents himself throughout the novel as the most reasonable person in the room: measured, curious, sympathetic to every perspective. He is also deeply afraid. Marković reveals his interiority gradually, in small increments, showing us a man who has built an entire professional identity on a falsehood he only recently discovered was a falsehood, and who has watched his options narrow with the methodical logic of someone counting the rungs of a ladder descending into darkness. The revelation of his guilt does not make Fell a monster. It makes him something more disturbing: a deeply ordinary person who committed an extraordinary act of violence because he could see no other way to survive.

**Marta Vukić**, Irena's daughter from her first marriage, arrives on the island with the contained, braced quality of someone expecting a fight. She is forty-one, works as an architect in Split, and has spent the better part of two decades navigating a relationship with her mother defined by Irena's absolute refusal to be a conventional parent. The conflict between them is not merely emotional but legal and material: Irena has repeatedly changed her will, and Marta has reason to believe that the estate — the island, the house, the significant royalty income from *The Salt House*'s many translations and adaptations — may not go to her at all. She is prickly, defensive, capable of sudden warmth, and so obviously a person of wound and grievance that every other character reads her as the obvious suspect. Marković uses this obviousness deliberately. Marta's pain is real, her anger is real, her motives are real and legible — which is exactly why she did not do it. She is the novel's red herring made flesh, the character whose guilt seems so overdetermined that the reader, at some level, begins to doubt it, and who in the final act becomes the figure through whom the novel's grief is most fully expressed.

**Dubravka Šarić** is the journalist, a sharp-tongued, fearless reporter for a Zagreb cultural magazine who has spent years trying to crack what she calls "the Vukić myth" — the carefully maintained legend of Irena's genius and integrity that the literary establishment has never subjected to serious scrutiny. She is thirty-eight, arrives with a recorder she is not permitted to use, and spends much of the novel fighting the obvious conclusion that she is the person Irena has summoned to destroy. There is, indeed, a history between them: Dubravka wrote a piece five years earlier that questioned the circumstances under which Irena acquired the island, suggesting financial irregularities, and Irena responded through lawyers in a campaign that nearly ended Dubravka's career. Dubravka is in many respects the novel's moral center — she alone is looking for the truth about Irena, though she does not yet know the full truth she is looking for — but Marković does not let her be simply heroic. She is also ambitious, sometimes reckless, and perfectly capable of using a person's pain as material.

**Ante Filipović** is Irena's former literary agent, now in his late sixties, who made much of his career on the back of *The Salt House*'s international success before a falling-out with Irena over foreign rights revenues ended their professional relationship. He runs a small agency in Zagreb, comfortable but not successful, and clearly views the invitation to Šipan as an opportunity either for reconciliation or for some kind of reckoning. He drinks too much, tells good stories, and radiates a sadness that the other characters mistake for nostalgia but is in fact guilt: he knew, or had strong suspicions, about the manuscript, and said nothing.

**Luka Petrović** is a twenty-nine-year-old novelist, Irena's most recent mentee, whose debut novel has just been acquired by a German publisher in a deal partly brokered through Irena's recommendation. He is earnest, talented, slightly overwhelmed by the company he finds himself in, and wholly unaware of the drama he has been invited to witness. He functions in the novel partly as the reader's surrogate — seeing events through fresh eyes unclouded by the long histories that bind the other characters — and partly as a kind of ghost of Branko Tomljanović, the man whose work Irena stole: a young Croatian writer at the beginning of his journey, oblivious to the debt the literary world owes to someone it has already forgotten.

## Plot Summary

The novel opens with the arrivals: the ferry from Dubrovnik bringing Dubravka, Luka, and Ante; Thomas Fell arriving separately by water taxi from Split with two heavy bags of archival material; Marta driving a rented speedboat that she ties off with practiced efficiency. Irena receives them on the terrace as the sun descends into the sea behind the island, conducting dinner with the formal precision of a conductor. She is charming, oblique, provoking. She makes a toast: *"To truths we have not yet told."* The line hangs over the evening. Ante laughs too loudly. Thomas refills his wine. Marta watches her mother with an expression that no one at the table can quite read.

The first full day passes in the golden languor of a Dalmatian September. There are walks along the shore, a lunch of fresh fish and local wine, a long afternoon during which the guests separately navigate their various tensions. Dubravka attempts to interview Irena and is deflected with elegant ruthlessness. Thomas spends four hours in Irena's study going through papers he has seen before, but this time he is looking for something specific — he found a reference in the Zagreb Municipal Archives two months earlier, a library acquisition record from 1988 that listed a manuscript titled *Kuća od soli* ("The Salt House") by one Branko Tomljanović, and he has been trying to determine what happened to it. Marta and her mother have a conversation that begins formally and ends with Marta walking away across the promontory, standing at the edge above the sea for a long time. Luka, innocent and unsuspecting, finds the oleander garden and sketches it in a notebook.

On the second evening, Irena makes an announcement that changes everything. She has, she tells them, been writing for the past two years: not fiction but a memoir-confession, a book in which she intends to tell the complete truth about *The Salt House* — how she obtained it, whose work it really was, and what she had suppressed and covered over for thirty years. She expects the announcement to be met with shock. What she receives, across the dinner table, is a collection of faces behind which different calculations are rapidly being performed. Thomas Fell goes very still. Ante Filipović pours himself a large glass of wine and does not drink it. Dubravka Šarić, who has been waiting for exactly this kind of opening her entire career, allows herself precisely four seconds of undisguised excitement before she reassembles her professional composure. Marta stares at her mother and says, quietly: "You're really going to do it." And Irena says: "I already have."

The night of the announcement is the last night Irena is seen alive. The other guests account for their own movements with varying degrees of convincingness: Ante claims to have gone to bed early, which cannot be verified; Dubravka says she was on the terrace until midnight working on notes, which Luka can partially corroborate as he saw a light there around eleven; Thomas tells the investigating officer from Dubrovnik that he went for a walk along the northern shore road before retiring, alone. Marta says she was in her room all evening, and she says it with the particular flatness of someone who knows exactly how guilty they sound. In the morning, just before seven, Josip the caretaker finds Irena in the oleander garden. She is lying on her back among the flowers, still dressed in the clothes she wore to dinner. The cause of death, initially ambiguous — she appears to have fallen, there is a head wound consistent with impact against the stone wall of the garden — will eventually be determined to be blunt force trauma followed by the deliberate staging of a fall.

The investigation divides the novel's middle section into a complex structure of interrogations, revelations, and shifting alliances. The mainland inspector, Šime Bogdanović, arrives by helicopter from Dubrovnik — a methodical, unhurried Dalmatian who has spent his career on the coast and is impervious to both literary reputation and the special pleading of educated people. He goes through the guests one by one. Dubravka, to her own frustration, immediately becomes a prime suspect: everyone knows Irena was attempting to destroy her professionally, and the motive of self-preservation is compelling. She responds to the scrutiny with a journalist's instinct for counter-investigation, and it is largely through her determined parallel inquiry that several crucial facts come to light.

Marta, who inherited her mother's intelligence without inheriting her mother's talent for concealment, quickly becomes the focus of the official inquiry. The will question comes out almost immediately: Irena had most recently signed a version that left the island and estate to a literary foundation rather than to Marta, cutting her daughter out almost entirely. The legal papers are found in the study, and Bogdanović notes with deliberate neutrality that Marta had ample opportunity the previous evening and that her claim to have remained in her room all night is uncorroborated. Marta's reaction to this — a cold, contained fury that reads as either guilt or the particular rage of someone falsely accused — deepens the reader's suspicion.

Ante Filipović begins drinking steadily on the second day of the investigation and at one point makes a half-confession that throws the investigation into brief chaos: he tells Bogdanović that he "knew things about the manuscript business" that he never told anyone, and that he had been afraid for years that someone would find out. This appears, for several hours of novel-time, to be a confession to the murder. It is eventually established that Ante knew about the plagiarism and had protected Irena's secret for decades, complicit not in murder but in the original crime of suppression. He did not kill her. He had, in a very particular sense, already done his damage thirty years earlier by saying nothing.

The pivot comes when Dubravka, searching Irena's study against the inspector's specific instructions, finds a folder in the back of a locked drawer. It contains Thomas Fell's own handwritten notes — not the biographical research notes she had seen him carrying, but something different: a letter, addressed to Irena, dated six weeks before the gathering on Šipan. In it, Thomas lays out what he has discovered in the Zagreb archives and the National Library in Ljubljana, where a second copy of Tomljanović's manuscript had been catalogued under a clerical error and never properly indexed. He tells Irena that he cannot publish a biography that suppresses this information. He asks her what she intends to do. Her reply, in her own handwriting on the back of the same sheet — Irena apparently typed nothing, considered email beneath her — is three sentences: *"I have already decided. You will see. I am afraid you will not like it."*

The final confrontation between Thomas and Bogdanović occurs in the olive grove above the estate. Fell, who has maintained his mask of mild cooperativeness throughout, breaks first not under accusation but under a single question: Bogdanović asks him, very quietly, whether he brought his archival bags from Split specifically to have a reason to enter Irena's study alone that second evening. The question is precise because it implies knowledge of a fact Bogdanović has not yet shared — that the archives in the study show signs of having been searched, that a specific folder relating to the Tomljanović manuscript has been removed and not returned. Thomas Fell sits down on the stone wall of the olive terrace and says, after a long silence, that he would like to speak to a lawyer.

## Mystery Elements

The central mystery of *The Oleander Garden* operates on two interlocking levels. The first is the straightforward question of who killed Irena Vukić-Horvatinčić. The second, and the more important, is the buried crime that preceded the murder by thirty-six years: the theft of a dead man's life's work. Marković structures her novel so that the solution to the first mystery is inseparable from the full revelation of the second, and the reader cannot truly understand why Irena died without first understanding why Branko Tomljanović died unknown. His death, in a Zagreb hospital in October 1987 of pneumonia complicated by malnutrition, is never dramatized directly in the novel — it appears only in archival fragments and Ante Filipović's guilty recollections — but its weight is present on every page.

The actual killer is Thomas Fell, and his motive is among the most carefully constructed in contemporary crime fiction because it is entirely without passion. Thomas did not kill Irena in anger, or jealousy, or even in the heat of an argument. He killed her in cold, administrative terror. When he discovered the Tomljanović manuscript — first the library acquisition record, then the manuscript itself in Ljubljana, preserved by bureaucratic accident, bearing on its title page the young postal clerk's name and address — he understood immediately what it meant not only for Irena's legacy but for his own. He had spent fifteen years building a critical reputation on the argument that *The Salt House* was the defining Central European novel of the last half-century. His biography of Irena, his lectures, his reviews, his entire position in literary culture rested on that argument. If Irena published her confession, Thomas Fell would be revealed not merely as a man who had championed a plagiarist, but as a man who had, in his exhaustive biographical research, had every opportunity to discover the truth and had either failed to do so through incompetence or suppressed it through complicity.

The red herrings are extraordinarily well-managed. Dubravka Šarić is the most obvious false suspect because her conflict with Irena is the most public and the most recent. The novel gives her a documented history of confrontation, a known professional motive, and even an opportunity: she was the last person, apart from the killer, to see Irena on the terrace the night of the murder. Marković spends considerable care making Dubravka's behaviour immediately after the murder seem suspicious: she is too calm, too observant, too quickly focused on investigating the other guests. The reader familiar with crime fiction will recognize this as the behaviour of a journalist processing trauma through professional reflex, but the first-time reader or the reader committed to suspicion will find it deeply incriminating.

Marta Vukić is the second and more sustained red herring, and she is the novel's most complex misdirection because her guilt, if it exists, would be so entirely justified. The will situation is damning. The long history of maternal cruelty is damning. The lack of alibi is damning. Most damning of all is the fact that Marta knew about Irena's planned confession and had, in the weeks before the gathering, consulted a lawyer about whether the plagiarism revelation would affect the estate's legal standing — a conversation the lawyer, cooperating with the investigation, eventually discloses. This makes it appear as though Marta had been calculating the financial and legal consequences of the confession and had decided to prevent it. In fact, she had been calculating whether the confession's legal fallout would give her grounds to contest the will. She wanted Irena to publish the confession. She was the only person on the island who did.

Ante Filipović functions as a third layer of misdirection through his partial confession and his documented complicity in the original suppression of the manuscript. Bogdanović, and the reader, are briefly convinced that Ante might have killed Irena not to prevent her confession but for the opposite reason — to prevent her from implicating him in the cover-up. This reading is made plausible by the novel's revelation that Ante had, in 1988, encountered Branko Tomljanović directly and had — whether through avarice, expedience, or simple moral failure — declined to press the matter when Irena offered him a larger commission on the foreign rights deals the plagiarised novel would generate. The discovery that Ante has been paying a small anonymous stipend to Branko's surviving sister in Slavonia since 1995 — what he privately calls his "tithe" — adds another layer of complexity to his character without exonerating him.

The moment of revelation is managed with unusual restraint. There is no dramatic confrontation scene in the oleander garden, no final speech of explanation. Thomas Fell's unmasking occurs across two quiet chapters — the letter in the drawer, then Bogdanović's single precise question in the olive grove — and the effect is of something collapsing very slowly and very quietly, like a wall giving way not with a crash but with a deep, grinding, irreversible sigh. What the revelation recontextualizes most brutally is Thomas's behaviour in the novel's first half: the careful reasonableness, the precise sympathy, the way he positioned himself as the most objective observer of the drama unfolding around him. Every instance of his measured fairness is retroactively revealed as the performance of a man controlling his own terror. His single moment of genuine emotion in the novel — a brief passage in which he describes his feelings upon first reading *The Salt House* as a young man, the particular joy of encountering a great work — is not cancelled by the revelation but made more terrible by it. He loved the book. He loved it knowing it was not hers. He killed the woman he knew had stolen it to protect the love he had misdirected at the theft.

## Themes

- **The ethics of literary inheritance and authorship.** The novel's central argument is about who owns a story and what it means to steal one. Irena's crime is not simply the plagiarism of a text but the murder — by negligence, by willful suppression — of a reputation, a legacy, an identity. Branko Tomljanović died without readers. His greatest work was published under another name and celebrated without reference to him. Marković refuses to let the novel treat this as an abstract ethical question: she gives Branko's life enough texture, through archival fragments and Ante's memory, that his loss feels specific and personal. The novel asks whether great art can be permanently tainted by the circumstances of its creation, and declines to answer.

- **Reputation as a form of violence.** Every character in the novel has done something to protect or attack a reputation — their own or someone else's. Thomas kills to protect his critical legacy. Irena spent thirty years managing the erasure of Branko's name. Ante's complicity was the complicity of a man who chose the reputation of a client over the rights of a stranger. Even Dubravka's journalism is framed as an act of reputational warfare, however justified. The novel suggests that the literary world's intense investment in reputation — in who gets to be called great, who gets to be called original — creates the conditions for exactly this kind of violence.

- **The corruption of mentorship.** Luka Petrović's presence in the novel is a formal argument about the mentor relationship: he is the young writer Irena chose to champion, and his gratitude to her is genuine and moving. But his presence also raises a question the novel does not answer directly: did Irena champion him because she believed in his talent, or because having a protégé served her carefully managed public image? And if she had lived and published her confession, what would it have meant for Luka, who had been publicly associated with her patronage? The novel implies that the corruption of the original act extends forward through time, poisoning even the relationships that appear to be genuine.

- **The impossibility of late confession.** Irena's decision to publish her confession is presented, by herself, as an act of conscience and courage. The novel does not entirely disagree, but it complicates the framing by asking whose interests the confession actually serves. It serves Irena's need for absolution. It serves Branko's memory, but only posthumously, and only in the diminished form of acknowledgment rather than the life he should have had. It serves the literary record. It destroys Thomas Fell, Ante Filipović, the publisher who has profited from the work for thirty years, and potentially the institution of Croatian literary culture that has organized itself in part around *The Salt House*'s reputation. Marković is genuinely ambivalent about whether Irena's decision was right, and this ambivalence is one of the novel's great moral strengths.

- **The specific landscape of the Eastern Adriatic as a repository of suppressed history.** The Dalmatian coast and its islands have been sites of occupation, erasure, and contested memory for centuries — Roman, Venetian, Habsburg, Yugoslav, and now Croatian. Marković uses this historical depth deliberately, embedding the novel's story of a single suppressed manuscript within a much longer history of cultural suppression, of voices overwritten by the voices of the powerful. The oleander garden, the island, the stone walls worn smooth by centuries of touch: these are not merely picturesque details but the physical residue of history, reminders that what is buried does not disappear, that it waits in the archive as a library card in Zagreb waited for Thomas Fell to find it.

- **The ambiguity of guilt in the face of complicity.** At the novel's end, when Thomas Fell has been taken from the island by Bogdanović's officers and the five remaining guests sit on the terrace watching the helicopter lift away, the novel refuses to resolve into simplicity. Everyone on that terrace is complicit in something. Ante in his silence and his tithe. Marta in her years of strategic indifference to her mother's moral state. Even Dubravka, who might have found the truth earlier if she had been willing to spend less time on legal exposure and more on archival research. The novel does not suggest that complicity is equivalent to murder, but it insists that Thomas Fell did not act alone, that Irena's death and Branko's erasure both required an entire ecosystem of people willing to not look too closely at what they already half-suspected.
