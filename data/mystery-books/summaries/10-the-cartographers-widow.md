# The Cartographer's Widow

**Author:** Anneliese Vogel
**Published:** 2023
**Genre:** Mystery — Historical-contemporary thriller

---

## Overview

*The Cartographer's Widow* is a novel that moves across two timescales and two continents, braiding together a present-day murder investigation in Amsterdam with archival flashbacks to seventeenth-century Dutch colonial rule in the Indonesian archipelago. Anneliese Vogel, whose previous three novels established her as a meticulous researcher and an elegant stylist, here produces her most ambitious and most urgent work — a story about the violence of cartographic authority, about the way maps are not neutral records of the world but instruments of power, and about what happens when a document that was supposed to stay buried resurfaces four centuries after it was drawn. The novel is a murder mystery and a legal thriller and a work of postcolonial historical fiction all at once, and Vogel manages these registers with impressive control, allowing each mode to illuminate the others without any one of them collapsing under the weight of the novel's evident ambitions.

The story begins with a death that almost no one investigates: Willem Drost, a celebrated historical cartographer affiliated with the University of Amsterdam's Bijzondere Collecties, dies of what his physician records as cardiac arrest at the age of seventy-one. He had been in apparently reasonable health for a man of his age. His wife, Liesbeth Drost-Van Kampen, accepts the diagnosis with a composure that those who know her attribute to stoicism. She is seventy-three, a retired archivist, possessed of that particular self-possession that belongs to people who have spent their professional lives in the careful custody of other people's records. She does not grieve publicly. She continues, in the months after Willem's death, the research he had been conducting — sifting through his papers, corresponding with colleagues, making inquiries at archives in Jakarta and The Hague. Six months after her husband's death, she is found dead in his private map collection, a locked room in the basement of their Herengracht townhouse that Willem had spent decades filling with seventeenth-century Dutch colonial cartographic material. Her death is initially attributed to a fall: the collection room has a narrow rolling ladder for accessing the high shelves, and she is found beneath it.

It is Liesbeth's niece, Dr. Rosa Drost-Vermeer — a geographer at the University of Amsterdam who studies colonial-era Dutch mapping of maritime Southeast Asia — who refuses to accept either death as natural. Rosa is not a detective. She is a scholar who knows how to read documents and follow sources, and what she finds when she begins to look seriously at her uncle's research notes is a thread that leads back to a seventeenth-century map of extraordinary provenance and explosive legal implications. *The Cartographer's Widow* is in large part the story of Rosa's investigation, but it is also the story of what that map is, where it has been, and why the men who hold power are so desperately afraid of it. Vogel makes of Rosa a compelling and fully human protagonist — anxious, brilliant, occasionally reckless, sustained by grief into a determination that transcends ordinary courage — and the result is a novel that achieves genuine thriller momentum while maintaining the intellectual seriousness of the best historical fiction.

## Setting

Amsterdam in the novel is both entirely contemporary and densely historical — a city whose present has been built so completely atop its past that the two cannot be cleanly separated. Vogel's Amsterdam is full of the sensory texture of a working city: the sound of cyclists on the cobblestones early in the morning, the smell of the canals in summer heat, the particular quality of the flat northern light that Dutch Golden Age painters captured so obsessively and that still falls across the facades of the canal houses with the same geometry it always has. Rosa's office at the university is in a building on the Singel that was a merchant's warehouse in the seventeenth century; when she stays late working she can sometimes hear, in the gaps between the heating system's noise, what she privately thinks of as the building breathing, the long slow respiration of old wood and older brick. The city is alive in Vogel's rendering, and it is alive precisely because it is so old, because the mechanisms of power and trade that shaped the seventeenth-century world are still audible in its architecture and its institutions.

The Drost townhouse on the Herengracht is one of the novel's most important locations, and Vogel renders it with the care of someone who has spent time in similar buildings. The Herengracht — the Gentleman's Canal — is the grandest of Amsterdam's ring canals, lined with the narrow-fronted houses of the merchants who ran the VOC, the Dutch East India Company, in the seventeenth century. That the Drost house stands here is not incidental: Willem Drost spent his career studying VOC cartography, and the choice to live on the street where the VOC's principal investors once lived was, Rosa reflects, entirely deliberate on his part, a way of keeping himself in physical proximity to his subject matter. The map collection in the basement is a revelation: a room whose temperature and humidity have been carefully controlled, lined floor to ceiling with steel-framed shelving, the maps stored in acid-free folders, the whole space smelling of conservation materials and the particular dry silence of preserved things. Willem spent thirty years building it. Liesbeth's body is found there at the foot of the ladder, and Rosa, entering the room for the first time after her aunt's death, has the uncanny sensation that the maps on the shelves have been watching.

The Indonesian sequences are set primarily in the port of Batavia — the colonial city built by the VOC on the site of what is now Jakarta — in the years 1647 to 1653. These chapters are rendered in a prose deliberately different from the contemporary sections: longer sentences, a slightly more formal register, a quality of painstaking observation that reflects the consciousness of the novel's seventeenth-century narrator, a junior VOC cartographer named Maarten Elzevir. Elzevir is a secondary character in the strict sense — he appears only in these archival sequences, which are written as if transcribed from his own journals — but his voice and his moral intelligence give the flashback sections a surprising emotional weight. Batavia in 1650 is a city of violence barely contained behind the façade of commercial order: the VOC's headquarters, its warehouses, its fortifications, all testament to the company's extraordinary power, while everywhere at the edges of Elzevir's careful accounts are the signs of what that power costs. The local population appears in his journal as a perpetual background presence, the people his employers are systematically dispossessing.

The third key location is the Archive, which in the novel means specifically the Nationaal Archief in The Hague, where Vogel's most important archival revelations occur. The Nationaal Archief holds the VOC archives — one of the largest and best-preserved collections of early modern commercial records in the world — and Rosa spends two extended periods there in the novel's middle section, working through boxes of miscatalogued material that Willem had identified but not fully examined before his death. The archive is rendered as a place of genuine intellectual excitement and genuine physical discomfort: the reading room is cold in winter, the boxes are heavy, the handwriting in seventeenth-century Dutch is frequently illegible, and the cataloguing system reflects the particular organizational logic of a bureaucracy that no longer exists. But Vogel makes the reader feel the peculiar thrill of archival discovery, the moment when a folder yields something that was not supposed to be there, that someone tried to remove and failed to remove completely, that has been waiting for four hundred years for someone to find it and understand what it means.

## Main Characters

**Dr. Rosa Drost-Vermeer** is thirty-eight years old at the novel's opening, an associate professor in the Department of Human Geography at the University of Amsterdam whose specialty is the cartographic history of Dutch colonial Southeast Asia. She is described by colleagues as formidably clever and personally somewhat difficult — prone to pursuing arguments beyond the point of social comfort, impatient with people who haven't done their reading, fierce in her academic convictions. She is also, in the novel's private rendering of her interior life, a person who has been shaped by loss: her parents divorced when she was twelve, she raised herself in significant part through the books in her uncle and aunt's library, and Willem and Liesbeth are not merely relatives but the people who taught her what careful attention to the world looks like. Their deaths, in quick succession, strip something from her that she cannot immediately name — not merely grief but a kind of epistemological security, the feeling that the people who understood her best were still in the world. What replaces it is the investigation, and Vogel traces with great precision the way Rosa transfers her need into her obsessive pursuit of the map and the truth about the two murders. Rosa is not a comfortable person to be, and Vogel does not pretend otherwise. She is impatient with the police inspector assigned to Liesbeth's case, rude to Hendrik Maarssen the first time she meets him without yet knowing what he is, occasionally manipulative in her dealings with sources. She is also brave, rigorous, and genuinely motivated by something that transcends self-interest: a conviction that truth about the past matters, that the living deserve to know what was done.

**Hendrik Maarssen** is sixty-two, a senior partner at the Amsterdam law firm Van Leeuwen & Maarssen, which has been the principal legal representative for a consortium of Dutch and Indonesian business interests contesting a land claim in the Sulawesi region of Indonesia. The claim, brought by a community of indigenous Bugis people, argues that a section of the Indonesian government's post-independence land registry is invalid because the underlying VOC-era deed was itself fraudulently obtained — and that a seventeenth-century map in private hands, which shows the territorial boundaries of a specific region as understood by the local population rather than by the VOC, constitutes material evidence of this fraud. Maarssen has been suppressing this map's existence for eleven years, having learned of it from Willem Drost in the course of what began as a professional consultation. He is a man who has long since made his peace — or made what he tells himself is his peace — with the gap between his professional conduct and his private ethics. He dresses beautifully, speaks with a considered precision that law school did not teach him but that he has cultivated over thirty years of practice, and maintains in all circumstances a quality of surface calm that his colleagues find authoritative and that the reader, once his guilt is established, recognizes as the calm of a man who long ago decided that other people's suffering was a professional cost rather than a personal weight. He killed Willem Drost by manipulating his medication in a visit that appeared to be a social call. He killed Liesbeth by staging her fall. Both murders were clean, patient, methodical. They were not the acts of a panicked man but of a man who saw a threat to his livelihood and removed it with the same deliberate efficiency that he would apply to any other problem.

**Inspector Fatima Benali** is forty-four, a senior detective with the Amsterdam police who is initially assigned to investigate Liesbeth's death as a probable accident and who over the course of the novel is pulled, partly through Rosa's insistence and partly through her own growing conviction, into treating it as a homicide. Benali is the daughter of Moroccan immigrants who came to Amsterdam in the 1970s, grew up in the Oud-West district, and has spent twenty years navigating a police institution that is more comfortable with her than it once was but still, on certain days, in certain meetings, reveals its discomfort. She is meticulous, unsentimental, genuinely funny in a dry way that her colleagues either appreciate or do not understand, and possessed of a well-calibrated instinct for the moment when a story that almost fits the evidence is being told instead of the story that actually fits. Her relationship with Rosa is the novel's central professional relationship: they begin as adversaries, with Benali frustrated by Rosa's interference and Rosa frustrated by what she reads as Benali's complacency, and move toward something that is not quite friendship but is mutual recognition — two women who are very different from each other and who share the same fundamental commitment to not letting a manageable explanation stand in the way of the true one.

**Diederik Hooft** is a fifty-year-old former graduate student of Willem Drost's who now works as an independent map dealer and consultant, trading in exactly the kind of rare early modern cartographic material that the novel's central map represents. He is the novel's most elaborately constructed red herring: he had a very public dispute with Willem five years before the novel's opening, claiming that his own research had first identified the Sulawesi map as significant and that Willem had taken his work and published it without proper attribution. The dispute was adjudicated by the university and found in Willem's favor, but Hooft believes the finding was wrong and has never stopped saying so. He is bitter, underpaid, professionally marginal, and clearly harbors a rage toward Willem that has not diminished with time. He is also deeply knowledgeable about the map trade in a way that makes him simultaneously a suspect and an invaluable source, and the novel exploits this dual function to considerable effect, using Hooft's expertise to advance the plot's exposition while keeping his apparent motive in the foreground of suspicion.

**Sita Wulandari** is thirty-one, a legal advocate working for the Bugis land claim case through an Amsterdam-based human rights organization called Grondrecht. She is the daughter of a family from Sulawesi with a multigenerational memory of the disputed land — her grandmother was born in the community that was displaced — and she has spent four years working the case through every available legal channel while the opposing side, represented by Van Leeuwen & Maarssen, successfully delays and obstructs at every turn. She appears in the novel first as a contact Rosa makes through the university's colonial history department, then as a crucial source of context for the legal and historical stakes of the map, and finally — in the novel's last quarter — as the person who is best positioned to explain what the map actually proves. Her presence in the novel is part of Vogel's deliberate structural choice to place the indigenous claimants not at the periphery of the story but at its moral center: the land claim, Sita represents, is not background context. It is what everything else is about.

## Plot Summary

The novel's opening chapter is an act of deliberate misdirection: it is told from Liesbeth's perspective, in present tense, in the weeks before her death, as she works through Willem's papers and begins to understand what he found. Vogel does not reveal what Liesbeth discovers — that revelation is deferred — but she does establish, with quiet precision, that Liesbeth is afraid. She has changed the locks on the map collection room. She has made a copy of a specific folder and sent it somewhere, an action whose destination is not specified. She has written a letter she does not send. The chapter ends with Liesbeth descending into the map collection room on a Tuesday morning in November, and then the novel cuts forward to Rosa receiving the phone call about her aunt's death.

Rosa's investigation begins not with the police — Inspector Benali, at this stage, is processing Liesbeth's death as a probable accident — but with her uncle's research notes, which she retrieves from the study of the Herengracht house. Willem was meticulous: he kept cross-referenced notebooks in the manner of an old-fashioned scholar, handwritten, organized by date and subject, with a personal indexing system that Rosa knows from childhood. It takes her two evenings to find the relevant thread. Willem had been researching a specific map — he refers to it throughout his notebooks as "the Elzevir chart" after its cartographer — that showed the coastal and interior regions of central Sulawesi as they were understood and described by Bugis navigators and landholders in the 1640s, before the VOC's territorial consolidation. The map was unique because it was made not by a VOC surveyor for the company's administrative purposes but by Maarten Elzevir, a company cartographer who appears to have acted outside his official mandate, working with local informants to produce a document that reflected indigenous understanding of territorial boundaries rather than colonial intent. Willem had located the map in a private collection — owned by a Dutch family whose ancestor had acquired it under unclear circumstances in the nineteenth century — and had been consulting with legal scholars about its evidential value.

The legal significance is enormous. The Bugis land claim, which Rosa learns about through contacts at the human rights community she encounters via Willem's correspondence, hinges on demonstrating that the VOC's original deed of territorial transfer was fraudulent — that it purported to transfer land rights that the VOC did not legitimately hold because the local population had never ceded them. The Elzevir chart, dated 1651 and bearing the signatures of several named Bugis figures, is the most direct evidence of what the land understood by those who lived on it actually consisted of, pre-colonial. In Dutch and international courts, it would not be conclusive — it is four centuries old, the signatories are dead, the chain of custody is complicated — but it would be enormously powerful as part of the evidentiary record. Rosa begins to understand that Willem had been planning to submit the map, with his scholarly authentication, as a supporting document in the land claim case, and that someone had reason to prevent this.

The Diederik Hooft strand develops in the novel's second quarter. Rosa tracks him down at his office, a cramped space above a furniture restorer in the Jordaan, and their first meeting is tense and unproductive: he is hostile, assumes she is there to relitigate the attribution dispute, and refuses to discuss the Elzevir chart. Their second meeting, arranged through intermediaries at the Rijksmuseum's cartographic collection, is different. Hooft is drunk when she arrives, not incapacitated but loosened, and he tells her something that neither confirms nor clears him as a suspect: he says that three weeks before Willem's death, a man came to his office asking about the Elzevir chart. He did not buy anything. He asked careful questions about provenance and legal status. He left a card. The card was from Van Leeuwen & Maarssen. Hooft did not think much of it at the time — legal firms dealing with historical property disputes often consulted map experts — but now, he says, he thinks about it constantly. He kept the card.

The Nationaal Archief sequences in The Hague are the novel's most technically demanding and most rewarding passages. Rosa has identified, from Willem's notebooks, a specific box in the VOC archive that her uncle had flagged as containing "miscatalogued material of potential significance." The box, when she retrieves it in the high-ceilinged reading room on a cold February morning, contains routine company correspondence from 1649 to 1655, largely administrative. But at the back, in a folder that has been placed rather than filed — tucked behind a set of freight manifests as if someone set it down temporarily and it was never moved — is a series of letters from a VOC director named Cornelis Hagen to his counterpart in Batavia. The letters discuss, in the indirect language of the period, the need to "correct the documentary record" regarding certain territorial understandings in the Sulawesi region; they reference a chart that was "produced outside proper company authority" and that needed to be "removed from circulation." Maarten Elzevir's name appears in one letter. He was not removed from his post. He was simply not permitted to return to the archipelago after 1653, and Hagen's letters suggest this was understood as sufficient.

Hendrik Maarssen enters the novel's foreground at its midpoint. Rosa, having traced the chain of custody of the Elzevir chart through three centuries of private ownership to its current holder — a retired civil servant in Leiden named Pieter Verhoeven, who inherited it from his mother and had no idea of its significance — contacts the Verhoeven family and discovers that the map was examined, independently, three years ago by a legal representative acting for Van Leeuwen & Maarssen. The firm had approached the family requesting a long-term loan of the map for "scholarly authentication," had been declined, and had departed without apparently pursuing the matter. What Rosa discovers next, through Sita Wulandari's connections within the Indonesian legal community, is that Van Leeuwen & Maarssen subsequently filed a challenge to the Bugis land claim's authentication procedures, specifically targeting the admissibility of documentary evidence from private Dutch collections — a challenge that would, if successful, make it much harder for any privately held map to serve as legal evidence. The challenge was still pending in court. Willem Drost, who had been in contact with Sita's organization and had indicated his intention to submit the Elzevir chart with a supporting authentication document, had been informed of the legal challenge by letter from Van Leeuwen & Maarssen three weeks before his death. He had written back refusing to withdraw his submission.

Inspector Benali, who has been running a parallel and initially skeptical investigation, arrives at Maarssen through a different route: the pharmaceutical record. Willem Drost's physician, reviewing his patient's file at Benali's request following Rosa's sustained insistence that the death was suspicious, notes a discrepancy between the medication Willem was prescribed and the level of that medication found in his bloodstream at autopsy. The difference is not enormous — not the level that would trigger automatic suspicion — but it is there, and it is consistent with a deliberately elevated dose administered over a short period. Benali cross-references the visitor records Liesbeth kept in her household journal — meticulous woman, kept everything — and finds that Hendrik Maarssen had paid a condolence call on Willem approximately ten days before his death. He had brought wine.

The novel's final act is a controlled convergence. Rosa and Benali, who have been working largely independently and with considerable friction, share information in a scene at Benali's office that is one of the novel's best: two intelligent women who have been approaching the same problem from different angles and have each found pieces the other lacks. Benali has the pharmaceutical evidence and the visitor record. Rosa has the legal chain from the map to the land claim to Van Leeuwen & Maarssen. Together they have Maarssen. His arrest, conducted at his office at the law firm on a Thursday afternoon, is reported in the novel only obliquely — Rosa learns of it from a text from Benali that reads simply: "Done." The final chapter is Rosa in the Nationaal Archief, a month later, completing the work her uncle and aunt died trying to finish.

## Mystery Elements

The central mystery of *The Cartographer's Widow* is not one murder but two, and the novel's structural sophistication lies in the way it presents them as separate events — a natural death followed by an accident — before gradually revealing that both are murders committed by the same person for the same reason. The fact that neither death initially triggers a murder investigation is not a failure of the police but a reflection of how carefully Maarssen planned: he understood that two deaths in six months, in the same family, of people of advanced age, would not automatically raise alarm if each death individually appeared explicable. The interval between them was calculated. He waited to see whether Willem's death would end the inquiry. When Liesbeth continued her husband's research, he concluded there was no alternative.

Hendrik Maarssen's motive is money and professional survival in proportions that are difficult to separate. Van Leeuwen & Maarssen has been receiving significant retainer fees from the corporate consortium opposing the Bugis land claim for eleven years. The consortium — a Dutch-Indonesian agricultural company with interests in the disputed region — had revenues in the preceding year of approximately 340 million euros. The legal fees were a small fraction of this; the land at stake was worth considerably more. Beyond the financial interest, Maarssen had in 2018 personally assured the consortium's board that the documentary evidence the Bugis claimants were assembling was inadequate and that the claim would fail. He had made this assurance knowing about the Elzevir chart and believing he had neutralized it through the admissibility challenge. When he learned that Willem Drost intended to submit the map despite the pending challenge, he understood that his assurance to the board had been premature and that if the claim succeeded — or even if it gained significant documentary traction — his professional reputation would suffer serious damage. A senior partner at a firm of Van Leeuwen & Maarssen's standing does not admit to having misjudged a case of this magnitude.

The red herrings are structurally sophisticated because they operate at different levels. Diederik Hooft is the first-level red herring: a man with an established, documented grudge against Willem Drost, specialist knowledge of the map world, and a connection to the Elzevir chart. He also, crucially, cannot account for his whereabouts on the Tuesday morning when Liesbeth died. The alibi he eventually produces — he was at a private sale in Utrecht — is corroborated only partially, and Vogel allows the reader to sustain suspicion of him deep into the novel's second half. His hostility toward Rosa, his initial refusal to cooperate, his bitter drinking: all of it reads as either guilt or the behaviour of a man so consumed by his own grievance that he cannot engage constructively with anything. The revelation that he too was afraid — that the Van Leeuwen & Maarssen card he kept was an act of self-protection, that he had been wondering for months whether he was being watched — recontextualises his earlier behaviour without entirely excusing it.

The Indonesian activist group, the Bugis land claim organization and its allies, provide a second layer of misdirection. They had the most obvious motivation of anyone in the novel to acquire the Elzevir chart, and early in Rosa's investigation it appears plausible that someone within or adjacent to the activist network might have been willing to use extralegal means to obtain it. Sita Wulandari herself, before Rosa fully trusts her, appears as a potential suspect: she knew about the chart, had been in contact with Willem, and had also been in contact with Liesbeth in the weeks before Liesbeth's death. The novel carefully develops this suspicion and then dismantles it, not by giving Sita an alibi but by showing Rosa (and the reader) that the logic doesn't work — the activists needed the chart to appear in legitimate legal proceedings, not to disappear, and the deaths of the two people most able to authenticate it were catastrophic for the land claim rather than helpful.

The moment of revelation is double: Benali's pharmaceutical discovery and Rosa's legal chain are both partial solutions that become complete only when combined. Vogel structures this as a deliberate argument about the nature of investigation — that physical evidence and documentary evidence must be read together, that neither tradition of inquiry is sufficient alone, that the truth sometimes requires two different kinds of reader working in parallel. When Rosa and Benali finally share what they each know, in Benali's bare office with the canal outside the window beginning to freeze in the February cold, the solution assembles itself not as a dramatic aha but as a series of incremental clicks — pieces fitting together with the quiet finality of a well-made lock.

What the revelation recontextualises most significantly is Maarssen's initial presentation. He appears in the novel first as a minor character, briefly mentioned in Willem's correspondence as a legal contact who had made inquiries about the chart. When he appears in person, at a professional reception at the university that Rosa attends, he is entirely unremarkable — well-dressed, measured, asking careful questions about her research in the way that lawyers do when they are gathering information under the guise of social interest. The reader, at this point, has no reason to focus on him. In retrospect, every word he says at that first meeting is a probe: he is establishing what Rosa knows, what she has found in Willem's papers, how far her investigation has progressed. He is deciding, Rosa understands later, whether she also needs to be dealt with. The fact that she apparently knew less than he feared, at that point, is the reason she survived.

## Themes

- **The persistence of colonial harm across centuries.** The novel's deepest argument is that the dispossession enacted by the VOC in the seventeenth century is not history in the sense of something finished and past but history in the sense of something ongoing, whose consequences are still being adjudicated in courts and still shaping the material lives of real communities. The Bugis land claim is not a curiosity or a subplot: it is the reason two elderly Dutch people are dead. Vogel insists on the causal connection between the original colonial violence — the VOC's fraudulent territorial expansion, Hagen's letters about "correcting the documentary record," the erasure of Elzevir's chart from the official archive — and the contemporary murders. The same logic of suppression that Cornelis Hagen applied in 1651 is what Hendrik Maarssen applied in the present day. The tools differ; the structure of the crime does not.

- **Cartography as an instrument of power and an instrument of truth.** Maps in this novel are never neutral. The VOC maps that Willem spent his career studying are documents of colonial administration — they show what the company needed to show, omit what the company needed to omit, and encode in their geometry a particular vision of property and sovereignty. The Elzevir chart is extraordinary precisely because it is a map made against the grain of its institutional moment, an attempt by one junior officer to record what the land actually was according to the people who lived on it. Vogel uses this contrast to develop a broader argument: that cartography, like all documentary practice, reflects the values and interests of those who commission and control it, and that recovering suppressed or marginalized cartographic records is a form of epistemological justice.

- **The violence of institutional respectability.** Hendrik Maarssen did not kill two people out of passion or rage. He killed them as a matter of professional management, the same way he managed difficult clients and unfavorable precedents. This is what makes him the novel's most disturbing figure: not his cruelty but his composure, the way his violence was entirely integrated into a worldview in which other people's lives were simply variables in a professional risk calculation. Vogel uses Maarssen to indict a particular kind of professional culture — the culture of the large law firm, the corporate interest group, the consultant who has long since learned to translate human suffering into liability exposure — that permits this kind of violence by making it seem like mere competence.

- **The ethics of archival work and scholarly responsibility.** Both Willem Drost and Rosa are scholars who work with historical documents, and the novel is partly about what it means to take that work seriously — to understand that a piece of paper from the seventeenth century is not merely an object of academic interest but a living document with ongoing implications for living people. Willem chose to submit the Elzevir chart as legal evidence rather than merely publishing it as a scholarly discovery. This decision cost him his life, and the novel treats it as genuinely heroic without sentimentalizing it. Rosa's decision to continue his work, in the face of clear danger, is presented as continuous with his: a commitment to the idea that archives are not museums but arsenals, that what is stored in them matters.

- **The gendered distribution of grief and investigation.** It is significant that the two people who investigate the murders — Rosa and Benali — are women, and that the two people who were killed were also a woman and her husband. The novel does not make heavy weather of this, but it is deliberate. Liesbeth is dismissed as a widow completing her husband's work; her own death is treated as an accident partly because she is an old woman and old women fall. Rosa's investigation is initially treated by the police as the interference of a grieving relative. The novel suggests, with quiet anger, that the systems designed to investigate violence are better calibrated to take seriously the deaths and investigations of men, and that both Liesbeth's murder and Rosa's inquiry were enabled and sustained partly by this structural blind spot.

- **Memory, documentation, and the question of what can be proved.** The Elzevir chart's legal significance depends entirely on a chain of authentication that spans four centuries. The document exists, but proving what it proves — that it is genuine, that it represents what it claims to represent, that the signatures on it are who they claim to be — requires the kind of scholarly labor that takes decades and that can still be challenged in court. The novel is fascinated by the gap between historical truth and legal proof: Rosa knows, from her reading of the chart and the archival materials, what happened in Sulawesi in 1651. Sita knows, from her family's oral history, what happened to the land in the generations after. But knowledge and proof are different things, and what Maarssen was ultimately trying to destroy was not the truth — which he could not touch — but the mechanisms by which the truth could be formally established. This is, the novel implies, the most modern form of colonial violence: not the denial of events but the obstruction of process, the weaponization of evidentiary standards against the people they were ostensibly designed to protect.
