# Perihelion

**Author:** Josué Ferreira-Sant
**Published:** 2035
**Genre:** Science Fiction — Cli-Fi, Hard SF, Multigenerational

---

## Overview

*Perihelion* is a multigenerational hard science fiction novel told across three timelines,
following humanity's response to a predicted solar catastrophe. It is structured as an
oral history assembled by a historian born on an orbital habitat, interviewing the last
survivors who remember Earth.

---

## Setting

**Timeline One (2061):** Earth. Dr. Amara Osei works at the ESA Solar Monitoring Station
in Tenerife, Spain. The sun is entering a predicted period of elevated activity, but her
models show something the standard forecasts have missed: a resonance cascade in the
solar magnetic field that will produce a series of coronal mass ejections of unprecedented
scale over 14–18 months.

**Timeline Two (2062–2063):** The evacuation fleet, assembled from every functional
launch facility on Earth. Commander Riku Tanaka coordinates from the Baikonur-3 command
centre and later from the lead vessel, *Continuance*.

**Timeline Three (2097):** Lagrange Station L4-Meridian, a habitat housing 340,000 people.
Blessed Okeke, now age 35, narrates from there — she was born aboard an evacuation
vessel during the transit and has no memory of Earth.

---

## Main Characters

### Dr. Amara Osei
Solar physicist, age 38 in Timeline One. Osei spent three years trying to get her cascade
model peer-reviewed before taking it directly to the ESA director. She is meticulous,
privately despairing, and publicly composed. Her character arc is about what it costs to
be right about something catastrophic. She makes the first public announcement of the
predicted CME cascade on March 14, 2061, and is immediately subjected to institutional
resistance, political suppression, and coordinated discrediting campaigns from governments
that cannot afford to acknowledge the prediction without causing panic. She is vindicated
eight months later when the first of the predicted CMEs occurs, exactly on schedule.

### Commander Riku Tanaka
Fleet commander, age 51 in Timeline Two. Tanaka is a former JAXA logistics director who
is handed an impossible task: design and execute an evacuation of humanity with 14 months
of lead time, limited launch infrastructure, and no political consensus on selection
criteria. He implements a lottery system with categorical reservations — scientists,
engineers, medical personnel, agricultural specialists, and then a general lottery. The
novel does not flinch from the arithmetic: at maximum sustainable launch rates,
approximately 2.1 million people can be evacuated. Earth's population is 9.4 billion.
Tanaka's chapters are the novel's most brutal.

### Blessed Okeke
Born aboard the evacuation vessel *Endurance* in March 2063, the first human to have
never touched a planetary surface. Now 35 in Timeline Three, she is a habitat
infrastructure engineer and the novel's narrator-assembler, recording testimony from
the surviving evacuees as they vote on whether to attempt Earth recolonisation. Blessed
has no emotional attachment to the question — Earth is an abstraction to her — and her
outsider perspective allows the novel to examine the evacuees' grief with clarity.

---

## Plot Summary

Timeline One follows Osei's three-year struggle to be believed, the political suppression
of her findings, and the chaotic 8-month scramble that begins when the first CME validates
her model. This section is the most intimate — it is about one scientist watching
institutional inertia cost millions of preparation-weeks while she is invited to committees
and asked to revise her confidence intervals.

Timeline Two is episodic and structured around Tanaka's decision logs. The lottery
is described with procedural precision. There are chapters about the people who were not
selected — entire communities, regions, and in some cases nations with no launch access
at all. There is a chapter told from the perspective of a lottery administrator in
Lagos who draws her own name on the last draw of the last round and cannot bring herself
to board.

Timeline Three is the novel's emotional resolution. Blessed's oral history interviews
reveal that most of the surviving evacuees want to return to Earth, now that 34 years have
passed and solar activity has normalised. A minority, mostly those born in transit or on
the stations, regard Earth as irrelevant. The vote is 61% to attempt recolonisation.
The novel ends with the first recolonisation probe entering Earth's atmosphere and finding
the surface not dead but transformed — the CME cascade scorched and irradiated the surface
but could not kill the ocean, and something has been quietly adapting for 34 years.

## Science Fiction Elements

- **CME cascade modelling:** Osei's model is based on extrapolated real heliophysics.
  The novel includes a technical appendix with the fictional resonance cascade equations.
- **Orbital habitat engineering:** The L4 and L5 habitats are described in engineering
  detail — rotation rates, agricultural sections, population density limits.
- **Evacuation logistics:** The launch calculations, delta-v requirements, and
  life-support constraints are mathematically consistent.
- **Post-catastrophe ecology:** The ocean survival and adaptive radiation described in
  Timeline Three draw on real extremophile biology.

---

## Themes

- The cost of being right in institutions designed to resist disruption
- The arithmetic of triage and who decides who survives
- Identity and belonging for people born without a home world
- Whether the obligation to return to Earth is sentimental or rational
