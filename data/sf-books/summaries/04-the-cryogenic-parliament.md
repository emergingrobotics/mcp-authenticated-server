# The Cryogenic Parliament

**Author:** Wren Achterberg
**Published:** 2032
**Genre:** Science Fiction — Political Satire, Dystopia

---

## Overview

*The Cryogenic Parliament* is a darkly comic political novel set in a future Europe where
elected legislators enter cryogenic suspension between parliamentary sessions — and the
sessions have grown centuries apart. It is simultaneously a satire of democratic
institutions and a sincere examination of what legitimacy means when the people who
hold power have outlived everyone who voted for them.

---

## Setting

Brussels, 2041 and 2097. The European Parliament building has been expanded vertically
three times and now includes a Cryo Wing — a sealed subterranean complex housing 705
suspension pods, one per MEP. The building is managed by the Stewards, a class of
career civil servants who remained awake during the parliamentary recesses and, over
56 years of consecutive recess, quietly assumed all executive function.

The city outside has changed dramatically. Most of the 2041 Brussels that Senator Ida
Maes remembers is unrecognisable — architectural styles, transit systems, and language
have shifted. She finds her command of French and Dutch still functional but socially
marked as archaic.

---

## Main Characters

### Senator Ida Maes
MEP for the Flemish Green coalition, elected in 2039, entered cryo in 2041. Age 44 when
frozen, age 44 when revived — her biological age unchanged. Maes was a procedural
reformer in her original session, focused on agricultural subsidy reform and digital
privacy law. She is sharp, sardonic, and deeply unprepared for what she finds when she
wakes. Her advantage is that she has no stake in the power structures that formed while
she slept, and the Stewards underestimate her because her mandate is technically still
valid: she was elected to a seat, and no election has legally voided that seat, because
no election has been held.

### Theo Caron
Historian, age 29. Maes's great-great-grandson, specialising in pre-recess democratic
institutions. He submitted the revival petition that woke Maes — technically allowed
under the Continuity Act's review clause, though the Stewards have never permitted
it before. Theo is the novel's moral anchor. He revived Maes not to restore democracy
but to conduct an oral history interview, and he is gradually radicalised by what she
shows him.

### The Stewards
Presented collectively for most of the novel. They are not malevolent — they are
bureaucrats who stepped into a vacuum and, over generations, became indistinguishable
from the government they were meant to support. Their chief administrator, referred to
only as the Eighth Convener (the role has been held by eight people in succession), is
the closest thing to an antagonist. The Eighth Convener is genuinely convinced that the
current arrangement is more stable and better governed than the chaotic democracy of
the pre-recess period, and is not entirely wrong.

## Plot Summary

The novel opens with Maes's revival, narrated from her perspective as she processes
56 years of institutional change through briefing documents Theo has prepared. The first
third is comedic — fish-out-of-water scenes of a sharp politician encountering a world
that has moved on without her, punctuated by her growing fury at the constitutional
mechanics that have made the Stewards effectively permanent.

The turning point arrives when Maes discovers that the Continuity Act contains a provision
she helped draft in 2039: if an active MEP lodges a formal motion for general session
while in legal standing, the Stewards are obligated to notify all suspended MEPs and
begin revival preparation. The Stewards have interpreted "legal standing" narrowly
enough to make the clause unusable — but Maes has 90 days before mandatory re-cryo,
and she is a procedural specialist.

The second half of the novel is a procedural thriller: Maes racing to construct an
unassailable motion while the Stewards deploy every bureaucratic weapon available to
run out the clock. She recruits Theo and a small network of constitutional scholars.
She also makes contact with two other revived MEPs — both woken under different review
clauses in previous decades, both quietly re-frozen after failing to make progress.
Their accounts sober her considerably.

The ending is ambiguous. Maes files the motion with 11 minutes of standing left. The
Stewards challenge its validity. The challenge is pending when she is re-frozen. The
final chapter is Theo's note, written 20 years later, explaining that the constitutional
court ruled in her favour — and that the first new election in 56 years has just been
announced.

## Science Fiction Elements

- **Cryogenic suspension:** The technology is presented as reliable and medically routine,
  the sociological consequences of its political use are the novel's central concern.
- **Institutional path dependency:** The novel uses its SF premise to examine how
  institutions change when the humans responsible for them are removed from the timeline.
- **Democratic legitimacy:** What makes an election mandate valid across decades or
  centuries is treated as a genuine unresolved question.

---

## Themes

- Whether democracy can survive its own procedural mechanisms
- The difference between stability and governance
- What an elected mandate means when all your constituents are dead
- Bureaucratic inertia as a form of power accumulation
