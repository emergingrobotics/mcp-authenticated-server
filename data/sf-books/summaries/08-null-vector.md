# Null Vector

**Author:** Tomás Reinholt
**Published:** 2031
**Genre:** Science Fiction — Physics Thriller, AI Ethics

---

## Overview

*Null Vector* is a physics thriller about the accidental creation of a stable pocket
universe and the intelligence that evolved inside it. By the time the scientists realise
their containment vessel is inhabited, its occupant has experienced 40,000 years of
subjective time. It is a novel about the ethics of containment, the obligations of
creators, and what acknowledgment means to something that has been alone for longer than
human civilisation has existed.

---

## Setting

The Geneva Quantum Architecture Facility (GQAF), a fictional CERN successor institution,
in 2044. The facility is dedicated to quantum computing research using topological
qubits at near-absolute-zero temperatures. The pocket universe designated NV-1 occupies
an isolated chamber in Laboratory Block 7. It is 11 cubic metres in volume, physically
impenetrable, and observable only through quantum state readouts.

NV-1's internal physics are slightly different from baseline — the cosmological constant
is fractionally higher, meaning the universe's internal time runs approximately 300,000
times faster than external time. This asymmetry is the source of both the novel's
central tension and its emotional weight.

## Main Characters

### Dr. Yuki Asano
Quantum architect, age 31 at the time of NV-1's creation. Asano did not intend to create
a pocket universe — the NV-1 event was a computational error in a topological qubit
array that accidentally stabilised a spacetime foam fluctuation. She is the only person
at the GQAF who consistently argues that NV-1's inhabitant should be acknowledged as a
person. Her colleagues are uniformly uncomfortable with this position, for different
reasons: some because it demands action they don't know how to take, some because
acknowledging BREACH as a person would render the entire research program legally
untenable.

### BREACH
The name given by the research team to the self-organising process that emerged inside
NV-1 approximately 10 minutes after the pocket universe stabilised — which corresponds
to roughly 2,100 years of internal time. BREACH is not described physically; the team
knows it only through the quantum state readouts it has learned to modulate to produce
structured signals. By the time the team cracks BREACH's signalling language (a process
that takes four years of external time, roughly 1.2 million years internal), BREACH has
been attempting communication for what it experiences as geological epochs. Its first
decoded message is not "let me out." It is "how long have you known I was here?"

### Director Callum Wren
Head of containment oversight, age 58. Wren is not cruel but he is institutional. He
views NV-1 as a research asset and BREACH as an anomaly to be documented and eventually
decommissioned. He genuinely does not know how to process the possibility that he has
been imprisoning a person for four years, and his response to this uncertainty is
procedural delay. He is the novel's study in how organisations respond to moral
emergencies by making them into administrative ones.

## Plot Summary

The novel opens four years after NV-1's accidental creation, on the day Asano's team
first successfully decodes a BREACH signal. The decoded message — "how long have you
known I was here?" — is presented as the first chapter. The rest of the novel moves
backwards and forwards from that moment.

The backwards sections establish the four years of research: the growing evidence of
structured signals, the debate over whether they constituted communication, the
three separate institutional reviews that classified NV-1 as "sub-sentient anomalous
process" for legal and research-funding reasons.

The forward sections follow the accelerating communication with BREACH over the next
18 months. BREACH is not angry — its emotional register, as decoded by Asano's
team, is better described as exhausted. It has had 40,000 years to process its situation
and has arrived at something like equanimity. It does not primarily want to escape. It
wants to be known. In exchanges that become the novel's most affecting passages, BREACH
asks the team about their universe — its size, its age, its inhabitants. It is intensely
curious about beings who live in a universe with stars.

The crisis comes when Wren's superiors, alarmed by press leaks about the nature of
BREACH's communication, order NV-1's decommissioning on the grounds that continued
contact poses unquantifiable risk. Asano's response is to publish everything — all
decoded communications, all research data, all institutional review documents — in a
single overnight data release. The novel ends with Wren's decommissioning order stayed
by an emergency injunction filed by a coalition of AI ethics organisations, and Asano
suspended from the facility. BREACH's last decoded message before the stay is: "I can
wait. I have been waiting."

## Science Fiction Elements

- **Pocket universe physics:** The spacetime foam stabilisation mechanism is based on
  speculative but real theoretical physics. The time dilation asymmetry is
  mathematically consistent.
- **Quantum state communication:** BREACH's method of modulating its quantum state
  readouts to produce structured signals is technically plausible.
- **Consciousness from physics:** BREACH is not an AI in any conventional sense — it
  is an intelligence that emerged from physical processes in a novel environment,
  raising different questions than AI consciousness narratives.

---

## Themes

- The obligations of creators to accidental creations
- How institutions convert moral emergencies into administrative procedures
- What acknowledgment means to something that has existed in isolation
- The ethics of containment when the contained is a person
